# How to Find Product-Market-Sales Fit

**Authors:** Jyoti Bansal (founder/CEO of AppDynamics, acquired by Cisco for $3.7B), Satish Talluri (A16Z), Peter Levine (A16Z GP), Sonal Chokshi (A16Z)
**Source:** https://a16z.com/podcast/how-to-find-product-market-sales-fit/
**Date:** September 3, 2022
**Format:** Podcast (47 minutes)

---

## The Core Concept: Product-Market-*Sales* Fit

The central thesis of this conversation is that "product-market fit" alone is misleading. Founders need to find **product-market-sales fit** — the right market, the right product, *and* the right sales/go-to-market strategy that works together. As Jyoti Bansal puts it:

> "In AppDynamics, I used to say, it's a little bit misleading to just call it product-market fit. We should call it product-market-sales fit. It's like, have we found the right market, and we have the right product, and we have the right sales or go-to-market strategy that works for it."

Peter Levine adds that the features of the product need to *inherit* part of the go-to-market within the product itself. If you're going after a certain motion or a certain customer, the product needs to be reflective of that — product features, interface, and design must be aligned with the target audience and target go-to-market.

## The Three Stages of Company Building

Bansal lays out a clear revenue milestone framework:

### Phase 1: $0 to $1M ARR — Find Product-Market Fit

- Figure out where the most pain is
- Validate that your technology really works and solves that pain
- Identify your target market: start broad, then segment
- Do people pay for it? Does it solve something?
- This is the phase of customer interviews, broad exploration, and narrowing focus

> "The phase one is really even figuring out where your target market is. You really want to start broad and then segment. If you don't know where would your idea or your product fit the most — is it large enterprise, is it SMB, is it financial services — then I would just go and interview all of them, and not narrow yet."

### Phase 2: $1M to $10M ARR — Find Product-Market-*Sales* Fit

- Iterate on the go-to-market strategy
- Get the product aligned with the sales motion
- Determine: does your product support the sales strategy? (e.g. freemium doesn't work if the product is too complex)
- At $10M ARR, you should have found product-market-sales fit

### Scale: $10M to $100M+ — Press the Gas

- You've found the fit; now accelerate
- Scale the sales organization
- Go execute on your go-to-market

> "The skills you need to master, $0 to $1 million of revenue, find the product-market fit. $1 million to $10 million, find the product-market-sales fit, iterate on it. $10 million to $75 million, scale the sales organization."

## The Sandwich Strategy (AppDynamics)

Bansal describes AppDynamics' go-to-market as a combination of bottoms-up and tops-down — what he calls **the Sandwich Strategy**:

- **Bottoms-up:** Developers and DevOps engineers start for free through a freemium/light version
- **Tops-down:** When multiple users exist in an organization, create air cover and sell larger enterprise licenses
- **Land and expand:** Small deals ($20K–$50K) on the phone via inside sales, then expand to large deals ($500K–$2M+, up to $10M) via traditional enterprise field sales

The model requires *both* inside sales (for the land) and field sales (for the expand). As Satish Talluri notes, for complex enterprise deals, services become an essential component of the expansion.

## Services: 10–15% Is the Sweet Spot

A counterintuitive finding: Bansal initially refused to sell services — not for margin reasons, but because he wanted the product to be easy enough that no one needed them. For four years, AppDynamics had zero services revenue. But as they entered larger enterprises:

> "We figured out — if they don't buy any services, sometimes no fault of our product — they just don't get that adoption that we want."

The right balance: **10% to 15% services revenue relative to software spend**. On a $1M deal, $100K–$150K in services dramatically improves adoption speed, reduces shelfware, and improves renewals.

Peter Levine frames the economics clearly:

> "If you think about, in that example — let's say services leads the buyer to purchase a million dollars of license, the blended margin on that is extremely high. Much higher than it would be on a $20,000 no-services deal. So while services from a unit economics standpoint may be a little more expensive from a margin standpoint, if it drives very large deals with software margins, you come out way ahead."

Bansal adds that renewals in years two through five are where the margin story improves: lower first-year margin from services leads to higher adoption, which leads to higher renewal rates, which leads to higher lifetime margins. And Satish Talluri emphasizes: "At the end of the day, adoption is what counts for a product. We hate shelfware."

## The 2/3 – 1/3 Rule: Engineering Investment Allocation

Bansal's rule of thumb for engineering resource allocation:

> "Two-thirds of our engineering investment should go with our existing TAM. And one-third of our engineering investment, we should keep putting on expanding our TAM always."

- **2/3** on the core: improving the existing product, adding features/capabilities for the currently addressable market, serving current customers
- **1/3** on TAM expansion: new use cases, new addressable markets, new users currently not served

AppDynamics systematically applied this for 7–8 years: started with Java monitoring, used 1/3 to add .NET monitoring, then used the next 1/3 to add the next adjacent market, continuously expanding TAM without disrupting the existing go-to-motion.

Critically, Talluri notes that during expansion, they kept the same target buyer and user so they could leverage the existing sales motion — changing both product *and* sales motion simultaneously "is almost like building from scratch, and that causes lots of disruptions."

## The Sales Learning Curve: Three Phases

Peter Levine and Jyoti Bansal describe a three-phase sales learning curve:

| Phase | Customers | Characteristics |
|-------|-----------|-----------------|
| **1. Initiation** | First 25 | Founders sell, evangelical selling, product management team sells |
| **2. Transition** | 25–100 | Trained salesforce can take over |
| **3. Execution** | 100+ | Mature sales organization, established references, Magic Quadrant presence |

> "The first 25 customers for the new products, we actually got the product management team to really sell it the way your founders will sell in a brand new startup — instead of hiring a new salesforce for that. But after that, our salesforce could take it."

## Startup Within a Startup: Selling New Products

AppDynamics learned this the hard way. When they gave their mature salesforce a second product to sell, the salespeople failed. They called the new product bad and wanted to kill it. Bansal realized:

> "When you have 100 customers and you have 50 references and you are in the Magic Quadrant for something, everything is well refined. And how you sell is different than when you're a brand new product with zero customers."

The solution: **Startup within a startup.** Create a separate evangelical sales team for new products, structured to sell the way the very first product was sold. Sales compensation is a key reason — salespeople can sell the mature product (easy) and hit their numbers, so they don't invest in the harder-to-sell new product.

Talluri describes their SWAT team approach for the fourth product line (Real Time Business Monitoring):

> "We literally constituted what we call a SWAT team. It constituted the product management, a couple of engineers, the best sales engineers, and one solution architect. We literally went and sold some of the top deals and created the sales enablement material, the market positioning."

Once the first 10 salespeople cracked it and made better money with better incentives, the rest of the salesforce wanted in. Levine advises leadership to actively promote successes: "Hey, did you know in the east region, we just sold new product X for $150,000?" Salespeople love recognition, and if the CEO makes a big deal about it, others want that recognition too.

A warning from Levine: **don't release too many products.** Every week a new product manager rallying the team around something new becomes distracting and expensive. "Don't let it get mainstream until you know it's gonna be mainstream."

## Pricing Framework

Jyoti Bansal's three rules for pricing:

### Rule 1: Can your salespeople describe it in half a sentence?

> "A customer is going to ask a simple question: 'How do you price your product?' And if you can't describe it in half a sentence, you have too complex of a pricing."

At AppDynamics, surrounded by complex monitoring of all kinds of systems: "We price by how many production systems you have." Simple, one sentence.

### Rule 2: Is it measurable?

Customers and salespeople must be able to measure it both pre-sale (how many licenses do I need?) and post-sale (how many are we using?). If it's not measurable, it creates friction in the sale and friction in tracking usage.

Peter Levine adds: "A lot of companies try to come up with a new model of pricing. So instead of price per user, or price per application, it's price for the number of air vents your server has. You have to come up with pricing that the customer is used to actually paying for."

### Rule 3: Price based on business value / ROI

> "I argue to most founders that you should price more than you think you should. You can always discount. If there's not value, you can always discount. And customers are not gonna pay more than what the thing they should pay anyways."

The mechanism: a formal ROI analysis in the sales process. AppDynamics had a "Business Value Assessment" (BVA) process. They asked: what is your current state costing you? What would the new state with AppDynamics cost, and how much money will you save? Price becomes a function of the savings and ROI.

Sonal Chokshi's summary: "Be creative with your product, but don't get creative with pricing. Do what you need to do that makes sense to the buyers."

#### Resisting Downward Pricing Pressure

When a competitor priced much lower, Bansal's internal team wanted to match. His response:

> "Do we really believe our product is superior? If our product is really superior, why would we not charge higher? We made a rule that we can always charge higher than them. If our product has superior value, and we know how to articulate and make the case about it, why won't we charge superior?"

## "How Would You Make the Business Case to Your Boss?"

This is the key question Bansal uses to reveal true product-market fit:

> "My favorite question to ask any customer was, 'How would you make the business case to your boss to buy this?' And that's when you would start hearing, 'Every time we have an outage, we're normally spending six engineers in a room for five hours. Now, with you guys, I can reduce it down to one engineer for 15 minutes. And that's my business case.'"

He connects this directly to the definition of PMF:

> "My simple definition [of product-market fit] is, if you understand the business value of your product, that's when you know. Unless you understand the business case, you don't really have a product-market fit because that's what you have to engineer your product around."

For pricing: "If your business case is strong, you will not leave money on the table."

---

## Don't Overthink Ahead

The closing advice from Bansal:

> "Don't overthink too far ahead. The skills you need to master change as you go. My advice to people — focus on the thing that you need to learn the most to get to the next milestone and excel at it, then worry about the next one when you get there."

Someone at $0–$1M ARR trying to figure out how to do product #2 is wasting time. The skills and organizational needs at $0→$1M are fundamentally different from those at $10M→$75M.

## Engineering and Sales: Breaking the Barrier

A cultural note from AppDynamics: they intentionally broke the barrier between engineers and customers. In Bansal's previous startups, "Engineers don't know how to talk to customers, so let's keep them away from customers." At AppDynamics, they did the opposite — since they were selling to engineers, it made no sense to isolate engineers from customers.

Satish Talluri: "Working with the salespeople who are actually on the frontlines to go and sell — for the engineers and the product managers, it really helps. At AppDynamics, you made a lot of engineers go on those calls, to literally understand who exactly is buying that product, how much is he going to pay, and what exactly you need to build."

## Product Roadmap: Three Competing Priorities

Once you have customers, the product team has three competing priorities:

1. **Win revenue today:** What features do we need to close deals and beat competitors?
2. **Keep customers happy:** What do existing customers need to adopt more, renew, and expand?
3. **Win revenue two years from now:** What new capabilities expand our TAM?

These map directly to the 2/3 (priorities 1 and 2) and 1/3 (priority 3) engineering investment rule.

For longer-term planning, Bansal describes working backward from a revenue goal: "Our path to $100 million revenue. How would our business look? How much can we do with existing products, and what new products will we need?" Then from $100M to $1B: they realized at least 40% of revenue needed to come from new adjacent products to maintain the growth trajectory. This top-down math intersects with the bottom-up 1/3 investment to define what gets built.

## Product Manager Profile

For technical enterprise products, product managers should match the target customer profile. At AppDynamics, where users were engineers, all product managers had engineering backgrounds. For a consumer fashion app, the PM should have fashion experience.

Satish Talluri describes three essential PM skill sets:

1. **Empathy:** Understanding the customer deeply, defining the product
2. **Business acumen:** How does it work with sales? How do you position value? How do you price it?
3. **Execution:** Working with engineers, hitting schedules, actually delivering to customers

> "There are very creative parts of product management. Then trying to come up with creative solutions is the second part, and then scaling the operation behind it — which is like a machine that can process the requirements from customers, from sales, figuring out the right pricing, packaging, all of that."

At scale, these skills rarely live in one person — different PMs with complementary strengths form a balanced team.

## Summary of Frameworks

1. **Product-Market-Sales Fit** — market + product + go-to-market must align as one system
2. **Three Phases:** $0→$1M (find PMF) → $1M→$10M (find PMSF, iterate GTM) → $10M→$100M+ (scale)
3. **Sandwich Strategy** — bottoms-up developer adoption + tops-down enterprise sales = land and expand
4. **10–15% services** — improves adoption, renewals, and blended margins on large deals
5. **2/3 – 1/3 engineering rule** — 2/3 on core TAM, 1/3 on TAM expansion
6. **Sales Learning Curve** — first 25 customers (founders/PMs sell), 25–100 (trained salesforce), 100+ (mature org)
7. **Startup within a Startup** — new products need separate evangelical team, not mature sales org
8. **Pricing** — (a) describable in half a sentence, (b) measurable pre- and post-sale, (c) based on business value/ROI
9. **"How would you make the business case to your boss?"** — the question that reveals true PMF and pricing power
10. **Focus on your milestone** — don't optimize for $75M problems when you're at $1M; skills are stage-specific
