# How to Get and Evaluate Startup Ideas

**Author:** Jared Friedman (YC Group Partner)
**Date:** November 2022
**Source:** [YC Startup School](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Th8JoIan4dg) | [Detailed Notes](https://www.mit.edu/~edchen93/posts/how-to-get-and-evaluate-startup-ideas/)

---

> No one — not even YC — knows for sure which ideas will succeed. And in any case, whether your idea succeeds has as much to do with how well you execute as your initial idea. But certain ideas are much more likely to succeed than others. My goal is to help you stack the deck in your favor by starting with a promising idea.

---

## Where This Advice Comes From

Jared Friedman's framework draws from four sources of evidence:

1. **Quantitative analysis** of the YC top 100 companies by valuation — how did billion-dollar companies actually come up with their ideas?
2. **Paul Graham's classic essay** "How to Get Startup Ideas"
3. **Years of helping YC companies pivot** mid-batch and learning which advice actually helps them find a good new idea
4. **Reading thousands of rejected YC applications** and identifying the mistakes that caused good founders to come up with bad ideas

---

## The Four Most Common Mistakes

Jared opens by identifying four ways good founders end up with bad ideas. The first two are about picking the wrong type of idea; the last two are about how you approach the search itself.

### Mistake 1: Not Solving a Real Problem (SISP — Solution In Search of a Problem)

The most common mistake is working backward from a technology to find a problem instead of starting with a real problem people genuinely care about. The failure pattern: "AI is cool. What could I apply AI to?" You end up with a superficially plausible problem rather than a real one.

If people don't genuinely care about the problem, they won't care about your solution. The fix: fall in love with a real problem first. Good starting problems are specific and tractable — not abstract societal challenges like global poverty, but something narrow enough for a startup to genuinely address.

### Mistake 2: Getting Stuck on a Tarpit Idea

Tarpit ideas are a recurring class of startup concepts that look attractive from a distance but have hidden structural reasons why they have never succeeded, trapping founders in months of fruitless work.

The canonical example: an app to help friends plan meetups more efficiently — submitted to YC for over 20 years with no success. These ideas are tantalizing because they address widespread problems nearly every founder encounters, but an invisible structural barrier prevents any solution from working.

The tarpit ideas form around problems that:
- Lots of potential founders encounter personally
- Seem like they could be easily solved with a startup
- But have a structural reason why they're very hard or impossible to solve

Before pursuing a tarpit idea:
1. **Google it** — find everyone who has tried it before
2. **Talk to those founders** — learn what went wrong
3. **Name the specific structural barrier** that stopped them. If you cannot name it, you probably don't have a plan to overcome it.

Tarpit ideas are not necessarily impossible. It's more accurate to think of them as common ideas that are much harder than they seem.

### Mistake 3: Picking the First Idea Without Evaluating It

Jumping into the first idea that comes to mind — without stopping to assess whether it could be a real business — burns months of founder energy on a bad foundation. The fix is not to be so rigorous that you become paralyzed (that's the opposite failure mode). Evaluate the idea enough to confirm it has promising qualities using the 10 key questions below. No startup launches with exactly the idea it succeeds with anyway; what matters is that the starting idea is interesting enough to morph in the right direction.

### Mistake 4: Waiting for the Perfect Idea

Founders who sit waiting for a perfect startup idea never actually start a company, because no startup idea is perfect at inception. The healthy position is somewhere in the middle of a spectrum between picking blindly and waiting indefinitely for certainty.

As Paul Graham put it: think of your idea as a good starting point. No startup idea is perfect at inception, and no matter what you start with it's probably going to morph anyway. You just want an initial idea with enough interesting qualities that it can morph in the right direction.

## Ten Key Questions to Evaluate Your Startup Idea

Use this as a structured checklist. A strong idea doesn't need to pass all ten, but each "no" is a risk to understand and account for.

### 1. Do You Have Founder-Market Fit? (Most Important)

If Jared had to pick one most important criterion, this is it: are you and your team the right people to be working on this specific idea?

**PlanGrid example:** Tracy had construction industry knowledge from years working in the field. Ralph had the developer skills to build an iPad app. Together, they were the obviously right team to build an iPad app for viewing construction blueprints. If you were going to imagine the team to start PlanGrid, the team you'd imagine would look something like them. That's what good founder-market fit looks like.

Reframe your search: don't ask "is this a good idea?" — ask "is this a good idea for *my team*?" An idea that is excellent for someone else but not matched to your team's strengths is not a good idea for you. Start your search by asking what you are exceptionally equipped to build.

### 2. How Big Is the Market?

You need either a large market today ($1B+) or a market that is small now but growing rapidly enough that it will be large by the time you scale.

**Coinbase example:** When Coinbase launched in 2012, the Bitcoin trading market was minuscule. But even then, it was pretty obvious that if Bitcoin succeeded the way people hoped, this would eventually be a billion-dollar market. Market size at time of entry matters less than market size at scale.

### 3. How Acute Is This Problem?

The strongest problems have no good existing solution — the alternative to your product is literally nothing.

**Brex example:** Before Brex (YC Winter 2017), if a startup wanted a corporate credit card, they literally could not get one. No bank would issue a credit card to an early-stage company. The alternative to Brex was not a worse credit card — it was nothing at all. That's what a good problem looks like.

Look for situations where users are working around the problem with painful hacks, or where an entire workflow breaks down without a solution. The more unavoidable and painful the problem, the less convincing you need to do when you approach users.

### 4. Do You Have Competition?

Counterintuitively, the presence of competitors is usually a good sign — it confirms the problem is real and a market exists. No competition often means no one actually wants the product.

What matters is whether you have a genuine insight the existing players are all missing. Entering a competitive space with a sharp differentiation is often an advantage; entering without a clear insight is the actual problem. Most good startup ideas have competition. When founders go into spaces with no existing competitors, they often find out the reason there are none is because no one wants the product.

### 5. Do You Want This? Do People You Know Want It?

If neither you nor anyone you personally know would use this product, that's a serious signal that maybe nobody wants it. This is a fast gut-check, not a definitive market study.

Founders who personally experience the problem they're solving have a natural feedback loop — they know firsthand when the product is getting better or worse, and they're motivated in a way that sustains the grind. It's amazing how often people start companies where the answer to both questions is "no."

### 6. Did This Only Recently Become Possible or Necessary?

A change in the world — new technology, a regulatory shift, a new behavior pattern — often creates a startup opening that couldn't have existed before.

**Checkr example:** The rise of DoorDash, Instacart, and Uber created sudden demand for high-volume, fast background checks on gig workers. Existing background check companies were built for slow enterprise HR workflows and weren't suited for this new use case. That is exactly the kind of change in the world that creates a new opportunity.

Ask: what has changed recently that makes this problem newly solvable or newly urgent? If the answer is "nothing," it's worth asking why nobody else has built this already.

### 7. Is There a Proxy for Your Idea?

A proxy is a large company doing something similar to your startup in a different market — it proves the model works without requiring you to prove first principles from scratch.

**Rappi example:** Rappi does food delivery in Latin America. When Rappi got started, DoorDash and other delivery companies were already doing very well in other parts of the world — they just hadn't caught on in Latin America yet. DoorDash was a great proxy to show that food delivery in Latin America would probably work.

A good proxy answers the investor question "does this model work?" with real, observable evidence.

### 8. Is This an Idea You'd Want to Work on for Years?

Passion at the start is a positive signal, but absence of passion is not disqualifying — it often develops as the business gets traction.

Many of the best startup ideas are in "boring" spaces like tax accounting software, payroll software, or compliance platforms where initial enthusiasm is low but business fundamentals are strong. Nobody starts off passionate about tax accounting software. But if you're actually running a successful business, you tend to become passionate about it over time. Don't let absence of initial excitement veto an otherwise strong idea.

### 9. Is This a Scalable Business?

Pure software scales infinitely — if you're building pure software, check this one off. The trap is businesses that feel like software companies but hide a service dependency: agencies, dev shops, or platforms where each customer requires significant human work to deliver value. If revenue grows proportionally to headcount rather than decoupling from it, you're building a services business, not a scalable startup.

### 10. Is This a Good Idea Space?

An idea space is one level of abstraction out from a particular startup idea — a class of closely related startup ideas like fintech infrastructure, vertical SaaS for enterprises, or food delivery services.

Different idea spaces have wildly different hit rates. Over the last decade, if you started a company in fintech infrastructure or vertical SaaS for enterprises, the probability that your company became a billion-dollar company was astonishingly high. In consumer hardware, social networks, or ad tech, the success rate was orders of magnitude lower.

A good idea space matters for two reasons:
1. **Idea spaces flip from hot to cold over time**, so pick one you expect to remain productive.
2. **If your initial idea is wrong, a fertile space lets you drift into adjacent ideas** without starting over.

**Fivetran example:** Fivetran (YC W12/W13) started making a tool for data analysis. They went to companies and tried to sell it — companies didn't want it. They pivoted and built a different tool for data analysis. Went back to the same companies — companies didn't want that either. But each time, they learned more about what companies actually wanted. Eventually they stumbled into an actual problem and an actual tool for data analysis that companies actually wanted. Because they kept shopping in a fertile idea space, they put themselves in a position to bump into a good startup idea. If they had picked a bad idea space, they probably wouldn't have found anything.

## Three Counterintuitive Signs of a Good Idea

Most founders shy away from these three types of ideas, which is precisely why they stay on the table for smarter founders to claim.

### 1. Ideas That Are Hard to Get Started (Schlep Blindness)

Paul Graham wrote about this in his essay "Schlep Blindness."

**Stripe example:** When Stripe launched, there were thousands of developers who already knew that integrating credit card payments was broken. They had tried to integrate credit card payments to their sites and realized the existing options sucked. But strangely, not one of them even tried to start Stripe. The reason: getting started required negotiating special deals with banks and learning the nitty-gritty details of credit card infrastructure. Those things seemed so hard that they scared off everyone else, leaving a $100B opportunity on the table for the Stripe founders.

If an idea requires an uncomfortable but one-time schlep to get off the ground, that difficulty is protecting you from competition. Ask "what's the schlep here?" before dismissing an idea as too hard to start.

### 2. Ideas in a Boring Space

**Gusto example:** Payroll software was obviously broken and widely used. Thousands of people must have realized payroll software sucked. But because it was boring, nobody tried to fix it until the Gusto founders came along.

Boring ideas have a higher hit rate than exciting ones (restaurant discovery apps, music recommendation apps) because they are systematically under-explored. Fun ideas get picked over; boring ideas get left on the table for a long time.

Here's the key insight: the day-to-day reality of building a startup — coding, debugging, talking to users, grinding on execution — is largely the same regardless of how glamorous the original idea sounds. Once the initial excitement of your idea wears off at 6 or 12 months in, how fun the initial idea sounded has little to no correlation with how much fun you'll actually be having working on your company. If the business fundamentals are strong in a boring space, that is exactly where you want to be.

### 3. Ideas With Existing Competitors

**Dropbox example:** When Dropbox launched, there were already about 20 cloud-based file storage companies. A naive reading: saturated, bad idea. A savvy reading: 20 companies exist and most people still don't use any of them — which means the problem is genuine and none of the incumbents have actually solved it.

Drew Houston had a very specific insight about what all of them were missing: their UIs sucked because they all worked the same way — you had to go to their website and manually upload files one at a time. Drew's technical insight was integrating directly into the host operating system to sync files automatically without the user doing anything. That was a step-function change in convenience.

The formula: competitors prove the problem exists; your specific insight proves you can solve it better than they have.

## How to Come Up With Startup Ideas

### The Organic Way (Best)

The best startup ideas are noticed organically, not generated deliberately. At least 70% of YC's top 100 companies discovered their ideas this way — not by sitting down to brainstorm.

When founders deliberately try to think of startup ideas, they tend to produce the same tarpit ideas everyone else produces. Organic ideas surface from real problems the founder has personally experienced or observed from a position of expertise.

**Three ways to set yourself up for organic ideas over the long term:**

1. **Become an expert in something valuable** — work at the frontier of a field. You'll see good startup ideas in that field.
2. **Go work at a startup** — you'll become an expert in what that startup does, putting you in a position to see adjacent problems.
3. **If you're a programmer, build things you find personally interesting** — even if they're not obviously businesses. **Replit** started this way: Amjad built it as something he personally found interesting, not as a deliberate startup idea. Sometimes things turn into companies over time.

## Seven Recipes for Generating Startup Ideas

Listed in order of how likely each is to produce a genuinely good idea. Start with the first ones.

### Recipe 1: Start With What Your Team Is Especially Good At

Begin your idea search within the domains where you already have deep expertise. Any idea you find there automatically has founder-market fit built in — this is almost like a hack to generate the set of ideas that has founder-market fit.

**Rezi example:** Rezi is like Opendoor for rental apartments. Before starting Rezi, the founders had worked in real estate and debt financing, and they were experts in those areas. When they got into YC, they spent the first month looking for ideas — but the smart thing they did was only looking at ideas at the intersection of real estate and fintech. That was a smart move because that's a very fertile idea space: many billion-dollar companies have come out of it. And because the Rezi founders were experts in it, their search was quick and painless. They came up with an excellent idea that has perfect founder-market fit.

It is weird how many startups apply to YC where the founders are legitimate experts in something, but the idea they're applying with is something completely different.

If you're a young founder (in college or just out), you may not have had a chance to develop the level of domain expertise the Rezi founders had. This recipe may not apply yet — try the ones further down the list.

### Recipe 2: Start With a Problem You've Personally Encountered

Ideally one you're in an unusual position to see. Problems you have lived firsthand are more likely to be real, and an unusual vantage point means the opportunity may have been invisible to everyone else.

**VetCove example:** VetCove is like Amazon.com for veterinarians — a website for vets to order supplies. The founders are brothers and their father is a veterinarian. Growing up, they noticed the way he ordered supplies was super old-fashioned: calling up suppliers on a 1-800 number. It was obvious you could build an Amazon-style website to replace that.

The VetCove story is a perfect example of what a great startup opportunity looks like:
- Thousands of veterinarians knew this was a real, annoying problem
- But veterinarians don't start tech startups
- Meanwhile, thousands of programmers in Silicon Valley were banging their heads against SISPs and tarpit ideas, totally unaware that over here was a genuinely great problem to work on
- When the VetCove founders got started, they had no competition. This amazing idea just got left on the table for years.

The intersection of "person who knows the problem" and "person who can build the solution" rarely overlaps naturally. If you sit at that intersection — due to family background, a past job, or an unusual life experience — that is a significant signal.

**Practical instructions for Recipes 1 and 2:** For each founder on your team, go through every job you've ever had, every internship, and every life experience. Think carefully about each: What problems did you encounter? What did you learn that other people don't know? What opportunities were you in a special position to see? Those are the best places to start.

### Recipe 3: Think of Things You Personally Wish Existed

This is the most intuitive recipe and the one founders default to most often.

**DoorDash example:** The founders were Stanford undergrads who wanted to order food from local restaurants and have it delivered to their dorm. Before DoorDash, you couldn't do that. So they built it.

**Warning:** This is also the recipe most likely to lead into tarpit ideas, because people wish for things that have structural reasons for not existing. Before acting on it, ask explicitly: **"Is there a reason this doesn't exist yet?"** If you can't answer that question confidently, dig before you build.

### Recipe 4: Look for Things in the World That Have Changed Recently

A major change — new technology, a regulatory shift, a behavior shift like COVID — invalidates old assumptions and creates new openings that didn't exist before.

**Gathertown example:** Gathertown builds a fun way to hang out with other people online. The founders were working on a different idea that wasn't going well. When the pandemic hit in 2020, they recognized that a massive behavioral shift had just occurred and that new categories of software were now needed, and pivoted accordingly.

Ask: what assumptions about how people live or work have just been invalidated, and what products become newly possible or necessary as a result?

### Recipe 5: Look for Recently Successful Companies and Find New Variants

If a business model has just proven to work in one context, ask whether you can apply it to a different geography, vertical, or customer segment.

**Nuvocargo example:** Nuvocargo is essentially Flexport for Latin America — they help US companies import from Mexico. Founder Deepak was working on a different idea at YC and realized it wasn't going to work. He went on a systematic search for better ideas and picked Nuvocargo for very analytical reasons: large market, good proxies (Flexport), and personal connections that would let him get started even without deep domain expertise in the import-export space. He felt he'd be very good at running this kind of operationally intensive business — and that worked really well.

This recipe works especially well for founders who are operationally strong and willing to learn a new domain.

### Recipe 6: Talk to People and Ask Them What Problems They Have

Systematically interview people within a promising idea space to surface real problems rather than generating ideas in isolation. This requires skill but can be extremely effective.

**A2B example:** A2B makes fuel cards — a special kind of credit card for truck drivers. The founders were young with little domain expertise, but they really wanted to do a startup with a genuinely good idea. Their approach was systematic:

1. **They picked an idea space:** software for the trucking industry. They picked it despite not being experts, because they felt it should be fertile: the trucking industry is large, hasn't been that disrupted by software yet, and probably had good problems to work on.
2. **They turned themselves into experts:** they physically drove to truck stops where truck drivers were milling about, walked up to them, and asked about their problems. They talked to founders who had started companies in the trucking space to get ideas for what problems were worth working on. They talked to anyone who knew anything about trucking and was willing to talk.
3. **They built a mental map** of the space — where the good ideas were, where the bad ideas were. They went through a whole bunch of different potential ideas before eventually deciding to work on fuel cards.

A2B is one of the best new ideas to come out of YC in several years, and the approach they used is something anyone could do. Most founders don't do this because it sounds like too much work. If you're willing to put in the work, this is an amazing way to find a startup idea.

**Best practices for this recipe:**
- First pick a fertile idea space, then talk to people within it
- Talk not just to potential customers, but also to founders of companies in that space for advice on what ideas are worth pursuing
- Don't talk to everyone — focus on a specific domain you've decided is worth exploring

### Recipe 7: Look for Big Industries That Seem Broken

Any large industry that is clearly malfunctioning is likely ripe for disruption — the combination of scale and obvious dysfunction is a reliable signal that a real, unsolved problem is waiting to be addressed. No personal connection to the problem is required.

**Bonus Recipe:** Find a co-founder who already has an idea. Startup School's co-founder matching tool lists many founders actively looking for a technical or business partner. If you have neither a co-founder nor an idea, this can solve both gaps simultaneously.

---

## The Bottom Line

It's often hard to tell if a startup idea is good or not. While these concepts help, typically the only way to know for sure if your startup idea is good is to just launch it and find out. If after all this you've got a startup idea and you're still on the fence, that is the advice: just launch it and find out.
