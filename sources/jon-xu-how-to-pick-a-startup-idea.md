# How To Pick A Startup Idea

**Channel:** Y Combinator

## The "Perfect Idea" Trap

Many founders get stuck trying to find the perfect startup idea before they commit. But the perfect idea doesn't exist in the abstract. The only way to find what works is to pick one, go deep, and get feedback from real customers.

The most important piece of advice: **Don't overthink it.** Overthinking takes two common failure modes:

### Failure Mode 1: Waiting for the Perfect Idea

Startups are hard, so shouldn't you figure out the best idea before committing? The problem: it's impossible to figure out the perfect idea in the abstract. You can only figure out what you should be working on by making contact with reality and getting feedback from customers.

### Failure Mode 2: "Am I the Perfect Founder for This?"

Founder-market fit matters. A non-technical founder likely won't be the right person to build a killer DevTools startup. But founders, especially second-time founders, weaponize this against themselves — convincing themselves they need a decade of domain experience before they can start.

The truth: you don't. If you pick an idea you're curious about, go extremely deep, and most importantly talk to customers, it's often possible to develop extraordinary knowledge in a short amount of time.

**Example:** Blake Scholl, CEO of Boom Supersonic. He spent his early career in adtech at Amazon and Groupon before deciding to commercialize supersonic flight. Lots of people thought he was crazy. Now Boom is a billion-dollar company.

Don't let the question of whether you're "allowed" to work on something stop you from starting.

## Why Working on Multiple Ideas Fails

Many founders work on multiple ideas at once, believing this is the best way to figure out which one will work. Two problems:

1. **It produces bad data.** If you don't go deep on an idea but juggle it with others, you won't get good signal about whether it works. You'll either prematurely talk yourself out of a good idea or convince yourself a bad one is worth continuing.

2. **The solution: go in-depth first.** If all your ideas look equally attractive, pick one and go deep.

---

## How to Actually Go Deep

### Burn the Other Boats

Explicitly foreclose your other startup options. Stop working on them. Tell any customers you've pivoted. Work with single-minded focus on the idea you've chosen.

Going deep should feel like **wearing a new skin**. You should become an almost unrecognizable version of yourself. This means changing your company name, emails, website, and even your internal narrative about why you're building a startup.

**Example:** GovDash, a startup that helps customers win government contracts. They pivoted at least five times before finding their idea. Each time they explored something new, they changed their company name, their mission, and even their email addresses — at one point the YC partner lost track of how to contact them. By truly becoming domain experts in government procurement, their fifth idea worked so well they could barely keep up with demand. They recently raised a Series B.

## The Litmus Test: Could You Run Your Customer's Business?

The high watermark for going deep: **could you actually run your customer's business?**

Say you want to build voice customer service agents for cleaning services. The question isn't just whether you've talked to 20 owners. The question is:

- If I dropped you into a cleaning business tomorrow, would you know how to run it?
- Do you know what their daily crises are?
- Is answering the phone a top-five problem?
- How much business do they lose when a call goes unanswered?
- What would they actually pay to never lose another one?

Another framing: **could you teach a class on the problem you're solving?** Are you one of the most informed people in the world on the subject?

Getting to this level involves lots of customer conversations and sometimes literally doing the job yourself. But don't obsess over needing to talk to hundreds of customers before writing code. The goal is to do both in a tight loop:

> Deep understanding of customer needs → product delivery → deeper understanding → better product delivery.

Real customers using your product produces concrete data that complements abstract knowledge, giving you a sense of whether what you're building actually works.

## Three Qualities of Good Ideas in the AI Era

Once you're going deep, these are the qualities to look for:

### 1. Sits at the Edge of What Models Can Do Today

Your product should barely work on today's frontier models, but will clearly improve as they get better. Understand the bottlenecks impeding your product's performance intimately. If a particular bottleneck doesn't clear the way you hoped, solving that might become the company.

This is a version of Paul Graham's quote: **"Live in the future and then build what's missing."**

### 2. It Should Verticalize — Sell Outcomes, Not Software

In the AI era, the cost of producing software is going to zero. What becomes valuable isn't "software for X" — it's customer trust, licenses, regulatory permission, and outcome ownership.

- Don't build software for insurance companies — **be the insurer.**
- Don't sell back-office software to banks — **be the bank.**

**Example:** Corgi Insurance (YC S24), an AI-powered commercial insurance company. They weren't content being a tech-enabled broker or managing general agent — that was only owning part of the solution. They set an ambitious goal of owning the entire stack from underwriting to customer service, and even acquired an insurance carrier during their YC batch. As a full-stack insurance company, Corgi can underwrite any insurance line in any vertical with a fraction of the headcount of traditional carriers — offering far better pricing, faster turnaround, and owning all the economics.

### 3. Aim at the Most Ambitious Version

The cost of pursuing a wildly ambitious startup idea and a modest one are roughly the same. They're both extremely hard. They both place extreme demands on your time.

So aim at the version that, if it works, **rewrites a sector of the economy** — because that's also the version that:

- Protects you from competitors
- Attracts the best talent
- Has a moat worth building

This could mean:

- Building and selling into the most regulated industries (legal, healthcare, financial services)
- Taking on very large incumbents (a $10B legacy SaaS company)
- Building hard tech (robotics for space assembly)

## What Happens When the Idea Fails

The good news: you'll be in a dramatically better position than where you started.

1. **Unambiguous customer data.** You know whether there's actually a hair-on-fire problem in this space or whether you just talked yourself into thinking there was.

2. **Real conviction for a pivot.** You'll have a better sense of how to execute going forward.

3. **A better idea underneath.** Most founders start solving surface-level pain points. The real opportunities are almost always the deeper structural problems. Going deep isn't primarily a process for validating the idea you started with — **it's a way to find the better idea underneath.**

This almost always happens, especially at the forefront of what models can do today. You'll notice bottlenecks, gaps, dev tools nobody's built — and one of those could turn out to be the actual company.

---

## Summary: Walk Fast in One Direction

1. **Stop trying to find the perfect idea.** Just pick one.
2. **Burn the other boats.** Learn everything about the customer.
3. **Try to execute for them.**

In the early idea fog, where you can only see 10 feet in front of you, the temptation is to take cautious steps in every direction — sample a little here, a little there, stay close to home. The problem: that gives you almost no information.

What actually works: **commit to one direction and walk fast.**

You're not guaranteed to end up in the right place, but you generate much more information per unit of time. And when you're walking, you might arrive at a better destination — one you couldn't have seen from the start.

**The worst failure mode isn't being wrong. It's not making a decision — spinning your wheels, dabbling between ideas, and never going deep enough on any one of them to learn anything.**

Pick one. Go deep.
