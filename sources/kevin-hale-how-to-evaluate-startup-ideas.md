# How to Evaluate Startup Ideas

**Author:** Kevin Hale (YC Partner, co-founder of Wufoo)
**Date:** July 2019
**Source:** YC Startup School — https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DOtCl5PU8F0

---

A startup idea is a **hypothesis about why a company could grow quickly**. Your job is to construct that hypothesis — basically, the pitch to investors — and break it down into component parts. Evaluating ideas this way tells you when to pivot, what's worth pursuing, and why you aren't growing.

This is a practical evaluation framework you can use before you build anything.

---

## The Three-Part Hypothesis

Every startup idea decomposes into three components. Investors listen for all three in every pitch.

### 1. Problem — The Initial Conditions

What is the setting that allows this company to grow quickly? Good problems share these characteristics:

- **Popular** — a lot of people have it (millions of users)
- **Growing** — more people will have it over time (market growing ~20% year)
- **Urgent** — people need it solved right now
- **Expensive** — costs enough that customers will pay to solve it (billion-dollar TAM)
- **Mandatory** — not optional; people are forced to solve it (e.g. regulatory changes)
- **Frequent** — users encounter it repeatedly (multiple times a day)

You don't need all six, but aim for at least one. If your company isn't growing or investors aren't excited, evaluate which characteristics are missing. The ACA (Affordable Care Act) is a classic example: regulation made a massive set of problems mandatory overnight, spawning a wave of healthcare startups.

### 2. Solution — The Experiment

**Never start here.** YC has an acronym: **SISP — Solution In Search of a Problem.** It's when you fall in love with a technology (blockchain, React Native, AI) and go hunting for a problem to attach it to. Companies *can* grow this way, but it's wildly inefficient.

Start with the problem people actually have, then use whatever technology is necessary to solve it. That sequencing makes rapid growth far more likely.

### 3. Insight — The Explanation

What explains **why** your solution will succeed and *why you will beat everyone else?* The insight must be connected to growth — it answers: "Why would an investor choose you over any other founder with the same idea?"

Your insight is your **unfair advantage.**

---

## The Five Unfair Advantages

You need at least one. Strong companies have two or three. The very best have all five. Each is connected to a concrete number.

### 1. Founder Advantage
**Are you one of 10 people in the world who can solve this?**

Being "a product manager at Google" or "an engineer at Microsoft" doesn't qualify — there are too many of those. A PhD with a specialized biotech patent and unique research *does* qualify. If you're not genuinely one of the few uniquely qualified to solve this problem, don't lean on this advantage.

### 2. Market Growth
**Is your market growing 20%+ per year?**

By default, building in a fast-growing market means you grow automatically — you're riding a trend. But this is the *weakest* unfair advantage if it's your only one. If you're in a stagnating or shrinking market, investors worry about long-term viability regardless of how good your product is.

### 3. Product Superiority
**Is your product 10X better than the competition?**

Someone should look at it and think: *"This is so much better than everything else I've seen"* — 10X faster, 10X cheaper, 10X simpler. A 2X or 3X improvement is nice, but not a slam dunk. An order of magnitude changes the conversation.

### 4. Acquisition Advantage
**Can you acquire customers for free?**

Paid acquisition (Facebook/Google ads with nice CAC/LTV ratios) is *discounted* as an advantage. The moment you become a significant revenue company, competitors enter and bid up your channels — see Blue Apron. The best companies grow through **word of mouth.** In the early days when you have no money, experiment with free growth. Things that don't scale today build the acquisition advantage tomorrow.

### 5. Monopoly / Network Effects
**Do you get stronger as you grow?**

As your company gets bigger, does it become *harder* for competitors to defeat you? Network effects mean the product becomes more valuable as more people use it. Marketplaces often exhibit winner-take-all dynamics. Not every company can build a monopoly, but when you have it, the outcome is outsized.

## Two Beliefs That Determine Success

Founders often fixate on the wrong question. Investors hold two distinct beliefs about every company:

### Threshold Belief
The minimal assumption that must hold for the company to have *any* chance. Typically: **"Can they even build it?"** For most engineering-heavy teams, the answer is yes — so this is *not* the question that determines the winner.

### Miracle Belief
The ambitious assumption that, if true, means the company achieves **breakout success.** This is what decides the lottery. Examples:

- For a deep-tech enterprise startup: the miracle might not be building the product — it might be *proving you can sell it.* Can you navigate enterprise sales cycles? Can you convince skeptical buyers?
- For a consumer social app: the miracle might be achieving network effects before running out of money.
- For a marketplace: the miracle is cracking the chicken-and-egg cold-start problem.

YC partners spend most of their diligence time probing the miracle belief, not the threshold belief. Founders should do the same: **identify what hard thing, beyond building, must be true for you to win — and gather evidence that it can happen.**

## How YC Evaluates Ideas: The Wufoo Example

Kevin Hale co-founded Wufoo, an online form builder. It started as a side project — building forms for people was tedious, error-prone, and required developers. The Wufoo hypothesis:

- **Problem:** Creating online forms was slow, technical, and expensive. Millions of people needed forms — businesses, non-profits, event organizers. Frequent and mandatory.
- **Solution:** A drag-and-drop form builder that required zero coding.
- **Insight:** Most form-builders targeted developers. Wufoo targeted *non-technical users* — a vastly larger market. The interface was 10X simpler than anything available.

**Unfair advantages:**

| Advantage | Wufoo |
|-----------|-------|
| Founder | Co-founders had strong design + engineering skills for UX-heavy SaaS |
| Market Growth | SaaS adoption was accelerating, online forms were becoming standard |
| Product | 10X easier than coding forms or using enterprise tools |
| Acquisition | Word of mouth — delighted non-technical users told their teams |
| Monopoly | Data network effects: templates, integrations, and form data improved the product for everyone |

Wufoo grew to tens of millions of form submissions, then was acquired by SurveyMonkey — validating the acquisition model component of the framework.

## YC's Own Growth as a Case Study

YC itself fits the same framework:

- **Problem:** Starting a startup was incredibly hard, and the best advice wasn't accessible. Founders needed funding, mentorship, and community but had no structured way to get it.
- **Solution:** A three-month program with seed funding, weekly dinners with expert speakers, office hours with partners, and a demo day for investors.
- **Insight:** The network is the product — being in a batch of ~200 startups creates peer support, credibility with investors, and a brand that compounds every cycle.

- **Unfair advantages:** Founder expertise (Paul Graham and team were uniquely credible), network effects (each batch made the YC network more valuable), acquisition advantage (word of mouth among founders), and a growing market (tech entrepreneurship was exploding).
- **Miracle belief:** That YC could scale from 8 companies to 200+ per batch while maintaining quality.

## Practical Evaluation Checklist

Run any startup idea through these questions before committing serious time:

### Problem Quality
- [ ] How many people have this problem? (Aim: millions)
- [ ] Is the number of people with this problem growing? (Aim: 20%+ annually)
- [ ] Do people need it solved urgently?
- [ ] Does this problem cost real money? (Aim: billion-dollar TAM)
- [ ] Is solving it mandatory (regulation, compliance, survival)?
- [ ] Do users encounter it frequently? (Aim: multiple times per day)

### Solution Check
- [ ] Am I starting from the solution (SISP danger)? Pivot to start from the problem.
- [ ] Is the solution at least 10X better than alternatives?
- [ ] Can I articulate the 10X improvement in one sentence?

### Insight & Unfair Advantage
- [ ] What do I know that others don't? Why me, now?
- [ ] Which of the five unfair advantages do I have? (Need at least one)
- [ ] If "founder advantage" — am I genuinely one of the top 10 people in the world for this?
- [ ] If "market growth" — is the market actually growing 20%+ year?
- [ ] If "product" — can someone look at it and immediately say "this is 10X better"?
- [ ] If "acquisition" — do I have a *free* growth channel? Can it scale?
- [ ] If "monopoly" — does the product get stronger as it gets bigger?

### Belief Audit
- [ ] What is my threshold belief? (The minimum for any chance of success)
- [ ] What is my miracle belief? (The hard thing that, if true, means breakout success)
- [ ] What evidence can I gather for the miracle belief *this week*?

### Acquisition Model
- [ ] Who would acquire this company and why? (Not required, but clarifies the value proposition)
- [ ] Is there a credible path to scale without paid acquisition?

## Key Takeaways

1. **A startup idea is a hypothesis about growth** — treat it like one. Structure it, test it, refine it.
2. **Start with the problem, never the solution.** SISP is the most common and avoidable mistake.
3. **You need at least one unfair advantage connected to growth** — being "pretty good" at five things is worse than being one-of-ten-in-the-world at one thing.
4. **Identify your miracle belief and go prove it.** Building the product is usually the threshold; sales, distribution, or behavior change is often the miracle.
5. **Good ideas often start as side projects** — Wufoo, and many YC companies, began as something founders built for themselves.
6. **Validate through customer conversations, not surveys** — real conversations reveal urgency, frequency, and willingness to pay in ways that surveys can't.
7. **Evaluate like an investor** — even if you never raise money, thinking like an investor forces rigor about growth potential, not just product quality.
