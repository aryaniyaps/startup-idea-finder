# The Only Thing That Matters: Product-Market Fit

**Author:** Marc Andreessen (co-founder and general partner, Andreessen Horowitz)
**Date:** June 25, 2007
**Source:** Originally published on blog.pmarca.com (now offline); archived at https://pmarchive.com/guide_to_startups_part4.html
**Series:** The Pmarca Guide to Startups, Part 4

---

This is the foundational text that introduced the term "product/market fit" into the startup lexicon and established it as the single most important concept in early-stage startups. Part of Marc Andreessen's legendary Pmarca Guide to Startups series, it remains — nearly two decades later — the most cited, debated, and operationalized piece of writing on what makes startups succeed or fail.

---

## The Setup: Team, Product, or Market?

Andreessen opens by observing two obvious facts about the startup landscape:
- There is an incredibly wide divergence of success — from insanely successful to outright failure.
- There is an incredibly wide divergence of caliber across the three core elements of each startup: **team**, **product**, and **market**.

He defines each term:

> **The caliber of a startup team** can be defined as the suitability of the CEO, senior staff, engineers, and other key staff relative to the opportunity in front of them. I focus on effectiveness as opposed to experience, since the history of the tech industry is full of highly successful startups that were staffed primarily by people who had never "done it before."

> **The quality of a startup's product** can be defined as how impressive the product is to one customer or user who actually uses it: How easy is the product to use? How feature rich is it? How fast is it? How extensible is it? How polished is it? How many (or rather, how few) bugs does it have?

> **The size of a startup's market** is the number, and growth rate, of those customers or users for that product.

On the common objection that product quality can't be separated from market appeal:

> Here's the classic scenario: the world's best software application for an operating system nobody runs. Just ask any software developer targeting the market for BeOS, Amiga, OS/2, or NeXT applications what the difference is between great product and big market.

---

## The Argument: Market Matters Most

Andreessen notes that most entrepreneurs and VCs will say *team* is most important. Engineers will say *product*. He takes the third position:

> **In a great market — a market with lots of real potential customers — the market pulls product out of the startup.**

The market needs to be fulfilled and the market *will* be fulfilled, by the first viable product that comes along. The product doesn't need to be great; it just has to basically work. And the market doesn't care how good the team is, as long as the team can produce that viable product.

Conversely:

> **In a terrible market, you can have the best product in the world and an absolutely killer team, and it doesn't matter — you're going to fail.**

You'll break your pick for years trying to find customers who don't exist for your marvelous product, and your wonderful team will eventually get demoralized and quit, and your startup will die.

## Rachleff's Law of Startup Success

Andreessen credits Andy Rachleff, formerly of Benchmark Capital, with crystallizing this insight:

> **The #1 company-killer is lack of market.**

Andy puts it this way:

> - When a great team meets a lousy market, market wins.
> - When a lousy team meets a great market, market wins.
> - When a great team meets a great market, something special happens.

You can obviously screw up a great market — and that has been done — but assuming the team is baseline competent and the product is fundamentally acceptable, a great market will tend to equal success and a poor market will tend to equal failure. **Market matters most.**

And neither a stellar team nor a fantastic product will redeem a bad market. As Andreessen puts it: "Markets that don't exist don't care how smart you are."

He illustrates the point with examples from tech history:
- **Great markets pulling out viable products:** Search keyword advertising, Internet auctions, TCP/IP routers.
- **Great products failing in terrible markets:** Videoconferencing (in 2007), workflow software, micropayments.

---

## The Only Thing That Matters

> **Rachleff's Corollary of Startup Success: The only thing that matters is getting to product/market fit.**

Product/market fit means being in a good market with a product that can satisfy that market.

This is the sentence that changed the startup world. Everything else — team quality, product excellence, marketing strategy, operational rigor — is secondary to this one objective.

---

## How to Recognize Product/Market Fit (and Its Absence)

Andreessen's operational description of what PMF feels like from the inside is the most vivid passage in the essay:

> **You can always feel when product/market fit isn't happening.** The customers aren't quite getting value out of the product, word of mouth isn't spreading, usage isn't growing that fast, press reviews are kind of "blah", the sales cycle takes too long, and lots of deals never close.

> **And you can always feel product/market fit when it's happening.** The customers are buying the product just as fast as you can make it — or usage is growing just as fast as you can add more servers. Money from customers is piling up in your company checking account. You're hiring sales and customer support staff as fast as you can. Reporters are calling because they've heard about your hot new thing and they want to talk to you about it. You start getting entrepreneur of the year awards from Harvard Business School. Investment bankers are staking out your house. You could eat free for a year at Buck's.

This is not a metaphor. It is an operational description: the constraint on your growth is not demand — it is your own capacity to deliver.

---

## Life Before and After PMF (BPMF and APMF)

> The life of any startup can be divided into two parts: before product/market fit (call this "BPMF") and after product/market fit ("APMF").

**When you are BPMF, focus obsessively on getting to product/market fit.** Do whatever is required — including changing out people, rewriting your product, moving into a different market, telling customers no when you don't want to, telling customers yes when you don't want to, raising that fourth round of highly dilutive venture capital — whatever is required. When you get right down to it, you can ignore almost everything else.

**When you are APMF,** you can begin to do the things that make sense at scale: optimize your core product for performance and reliability, hire specialists to increase your efficiency in areas that have become bottlenecks, and make strategic investments that build moats around what is already working.

Andreessen observes a pattern:

> Whenever you see a successful startup, you see one that has reached product/market fit — and usually along the way screwed up all kinds of other things, from channel model to pipeline development strategy to marketing plan to press relations to compensation policies to the CEO sleeping with the venture capitalist. And the startup is still successful.

Conversely:

> You see a surprising number of really well-run startups that have all aspects of operations completely buttoned down, HR policies in place, great sales model, thoroughly thought-through marketing plan, great interview processes, outstanding catered food, 30" monitors for all the programmers, top tier VCs on the board — heading straight off a cliff due to not ever finding product/market fit.

## Can Great Products Create New Markets?

Andreessen addresses the objection that a sufficiently great product can create its own market:

> Absolutely. This is a best case scenario, though.

He cites VMware as the most recent example at the time — a product so profoundly transformative that it catalyzed a whole new movement toward operating system virtualization, which became a monster market. But he cautions:

> Short of that, I wouldn't count on your product creating a new market from scratch.

---

## What a Great Team Actually Buys You

Since team is what founders have the most control over at the start, Andreessen addresses what a great team actually delivers:

- Hopefully, at least an OK product, and ideally a great product. But great products are really, really hard to build.
- Hopefully, a great market — but markets that don't exist don't care how smart you are.

He notes a recurring failure pattern: the second- or third-time entrepreneur whose first company was a huge success, who gets cocky and burns through enormous amounts of capital building for a market that doesn't exist.

Conversely, he can name "any number of weak teams whose startups were highly successful due to explosively large markets for what they were doing."

To quote Tim Shephard: **"A great team is a team that will always beat a mediocre team, given the same market and product."**

---

## The Meta-Observation: Founders Don't Know What Made Them Successful

> Ironically, once a startup is successful, and you ask the founders what made it successful, they will usually cite all kinds of things that had nothing to do with it. People are terrible at understanding causation. But in almost every case, the cause was actually product/market fit.

Because, really, what else could it possibly be?

## Legacy: Why This Essay Matters

This 2007 blog post did something unprecedented: it gave the startup world a single, clear concept to organize around. Before Andreessen's essay, founders talked about "traction," "growth," or "finding a need." After it, they talked about **product/market fit**.

The essay's influence is everywhere:
- **Y Combinator** built its entire philosophy around helping founders find PMF before scaling. Paul Graham's "make something people want" is effectively the same idea, operationalized through a program. Michael Seibel, former YC CEO, has said this essay was the single most influential piece of writing for him as an entrepreneur.
- **The Lean Startup** movement, popularized by Eric Ries and Steve Blank, is fundamentally a methodology for systematically searching for PMF before scaling.
- **Investor diligence** now revolves around PMF signals: retention curves, organic growth rates, NPS scores, and the Sean Ellis survey.
- **Every startup advisor, accelerator, and MBA course** on entrepreneurship now teaches PMF as the central organizing principle of the early-stage journey.

The essay also seeded its own evolution. Andreessen ended it with an editorial note acknowledging it raised more questions than it answered — how exactly do you get to PMF? How do you evaluate markets? What makes a product "fit"? These questions spawned an entire industry of frameworks, methodologies, and measurement tools.

## The Modern Operationalization: Sean Ellis and the 40% Rule

One of the most important answers to Andreessen's open questions came from Sean Ellis, the marketer who led early growth at Dropbox, LogMeIn, and Eventbrite, and who later coined the term "growth hacker."

Andreessen's description of PMF was vivid, but it was a **lagging indicator** — by the time investment bankers are staking out your house, you already have PMF. Ellis discovered a **leading indicator**:

> Just ask users "How would you feel if you could no longer use the product?" and measure the percentage who answer "Very disappointed."

After benchmarking nearly a hundred startups, Ellis found that the **magic number is 40%**. Companies that struggled to find growth almost always had less than 40% of users respond "very disappointed," whereas companies with strong traction almost always exceeded that threshold.

The survey questions Ellis recommends, which have become the industry standard:

1. **How would you feel if you could no longer use [product]?**
   - Very disappointed
   - Somewhat disappointed
   - Not disappointed (it isn't really that useful)
   - N/A — I no longer use [product]

2. **What type of people do you think would most benefit from [product]?**

3. **What is the main benefit you receive from [product]?**

4. **How can we improve [product] for you?**

The target: send this survey to users who have experienced the core of your product (typically those who have used it at least twice in the last two weeks). If 40% or more answer "Very disappointed," you have PMF.

### The Superhuman Case Study: From 22% to 58%

Rahul Vohra, founder and CEO of Superhuman, turned Ellis's survey into a systematic **product-market fit engine** — the most detailed public case study of operationalizing Andreessen's concept.

In 2017, after two years of building without launching, Superhuman ran the Ellis survey. The result: **only 22%** of users would be very disappointed without the product. Vohra had a number he could show his team, and more importantly, a framework to increase it.

**The four-step PMF engine:**

**Step 1: Segment to find your supporters.** Group survey responses by their answer to the "very disappointed" question. Identify the personas that appear in the "very disappointed" group — these are your high-expectation customers (HXCs), a concept Vohra adapted from brand strategist Julie Supan. Narrow your market to serve these users better than anyone else. For Superhuman, segmenting by these personas immediately raised the PMF score from 22% to 33%.

**Step 2: Analyze feedback to convert on-the-fence users into fanatics.** Study what the "very disappointed" users love (for Superhuman: speed, keyboard shortcuts, focus). Then look at the "somewhat disappointed" users who cite the same main benefit — these are the users on the fence who can be converted. Politely disregard users who would not be disappointed at all: "They are so far from loving you that they are essentially a lost cause."

**Step 3: Build your roadmap with a split focus.** Spend half your roadmap doubling down on what users already love, and the other half addressing what holds the on-the-fence users back. If you only double down, your PMF score won't increase. If you only address gaps, competitors will overtake you.

**Step 4: Repeat and make PMF score your most important metric.** Track the "very disappointed" percentage weekly, monthly, and quarterly. Rebuild your roadmap every quarter using this process. Within three quarters, Superhuman went from 22% to 58% — comfortably past the 40% PMF threshold.

The Superhuman case also validates Andreessen's original BPMF/APMF framework: once Superhuman crossed 40%, Vohra's advice was to "push the pedal all the way down and grow as fast as you can."

### Validation: Hiten Shah's Slack Survey

In 2015, Hiten Shah ran Ellis's survey on 731 Slack users as an open research project. **51%** of users said they would be very disappointed without Slack, confirming that Slack had indeed achieved PMF — with roughly half a million paying users at the time. This example demonstrates just how hard it is to beat the 40% benchmark, even for a product now considered one of the great startup success stories of its generation.

## Connecting the Threads: From Andreessen to Today

The arc from Andreessen's 2007 essay to the Ellis survey to the Superhuman engine represents a complete evolution of PMF thinking:

| Era | Contribution | Key Insight |
|-----|-------------|-------------|
| **2007 — Andreessen** | Defines PMF, establishes market primacy | "The only thing that matters is getting to product/market fit." |
| **2010 — Sean Ellis** | Creates a leading indicator for PMF | "40% very disappointed = PMF." |
| **2018 — Rahul Vohra / Superhuman** | Builds a systematic engine to increase PMF | "Measure it, segment it, double down on what users love, fix what holds others back." |

Andreessen's original essay remains the bedrock. It introduced not just a term but a framework for thinking about the startup journey — one that has shaped how founders build, how investors evaluate, and how the entire ecosystem understands what it means to go from zero to one.
