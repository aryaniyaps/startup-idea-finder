# The Goals of a Pre-Launch Startup

**Source:** https://www.startuparchive.org/p/former-yc-ceo-michael-seibel-explains-the-goals-of-a-pre-launch-startup

**Author:** Michael Seibel

**Date:** June 2024

---

## Introduction

Michael Seibel, former CEO of Y Combinator and co-founder of Twitch, has observed thousands of startups at their earliest stages. In a presentation on how to plan an MVP, he distilled the pre-launch phase into a sequence of goals that cut against the instincts of most founders. The core tension he identifies is this: founders want to build something polished and complete before showing it to anyone, but the pre-launch phase is not about building — it is about learning. Every day spent refining a product in isolation is a day spent betting that the idea in your head matches what customers actually want. Seibel's framework is a direct challenge to that bet.

## Goal 1: Launch Quickly

> "This is something that's been part of the YC ethos from the very beginning. It's been great advice for 10 years, and it continues to be great advice. If you walk away with one thing from this presentation, it's launch something bad quickly."

Launching quickly is the first goal because it is the gateway to everything else. Without a launch, there are no users. Without users, there is no feedback. Without feedback, founders are building in the dark.

The phrase "launch something bad" is deliberately provocative. Seibel is not advocating for shipping broken software that harms users. He is attacking the instinct to delay launch until the product feels complete — until the design is polished, the feature set is full, and every edge case is handled. That instinct is a form of risk avoidance dressed up as craftsmanship. Founders tell themselves they are being thorough when they are actually being scared.

Launching something bad means launching something minimal that solves a real problem for a real person, even if the implementation is rough. It means being embarrassed by your first version. The embarrassment is a signal that you shipped early enough to learn something. The founders who wait until they are proud of their product have waited too long — they have invested months of effort into assumptions that may be wrong, and the sunk cost makes them resistant to the feedback that could save them.

Seibel notes that this advice has held for over a decade because the underlying dynamic does not change: the faster you get something in front of users, the faster you discover which parts of your vision are real and which parts are fantasy.

## Goal 2: Get Some Initial Customers

> "Get anyone interacting with your product and seeing if they can get value out of it. You'd be surprised at how many founders' journeys end before a single user has actually interacted with a product they've created. It's very, very common. So please get past this step. It's extremely important."

Once a product is launched, the next goal is deceptively simple: get someone to use it. Not thousands of people. Not a cohort that produces statistically significant results. Anyone.

Seibel makes an observation that should trouble every founder: a startling number of startup journeys end before a single user ever touches the product. The founder has spent months — sometimes years — building something, and the story ends at the moment of truth. No user ever logged in. No customer ever tried the core workflow. The entire enterprise was a thought experiment that never collided with reality.

This happens for several reasons. Some founders launch into a void and get discouraged when users do not appear organically. Others interpret the lack of immediate traction as a verdict on the idea rather than a signal that they need to do the unglamorous work of manually recruiting early users. Still others never launch at all, trapped in a cycle of adding features that they imagine will make the product more compelling.

Seibel's point is that getting initial customers is not a growth exercise — it is an existence exercise. You need to verify that another human being, unaffiliated with your company and unburdened by your enthusiasm, can derive value from what you have built. Until that happens, you do not have a startup. You have a project. Crossing that line — from zero users to one — is the most important transition in the life of an early-stage company, and founders should treat it with the urgency it deserves.

## Goal 3: Talk to Your Users

> "This one is also an extremely common mistake because most founders have in their heads an idea of what they want to build. So they have this weird feeling that if they haven't built the full thing, getting feedback on the initial thing is kind of useless."

Once you have users, you must talk to them. This sounds obvious, but Seibel identifies a psychological barrier that makes it extraordinarily difficult in practice.

Most founders carry a complete vision in their heads. They know what the finished product is supposed to look like, and against that vision, the current version feels crude and incomplete. When a user gives feedback on the crude version, the founder dismisses it: "Of course they don't like it — I haven't built the real thing yet. Their feedback would be different if they could see what I see."

This reasoning is seductive because it contains a grain of truth. Some feedback really would change if the product were more complete. But Seibel's point is that much of the feedback would not change — and the feedback that would remain constant is exactly the feedback founders most need to hear. If users do not care about the problem you are solving, no amount of additional features will make them care. If they care about the problem but find your approach fundamentally misaligned with how they work, polishing the UI will not fix the misalignment.

The idea in your head might be something customers do not want at all. Talking to users early surfaces that possibility before you have spent a year building the wrong thing. Even when the core idea is sound, early conversations reveal which parts of your vision matter to users and which parts are solutions in search of a problem — features that exist only because they seemed clever to you.

## The Screwdriver Analogy

Seibel's most memorable framework for the pre-launch phase is a simple analogy:

> "Hold the problem you're solving tightly. Hold the customer tightly. Hold the solution you're building loosely."

Imagine you set out to build a screwdriver for a mechanic. You study the mechanic's workflow, you design a handle, you select a material for the shaft, you prototype a grip. You hand the mechanic your screwdriver. It does not work well — maybe the handle is uncomfortable, maybe the tip strips screws, maybe it is the wrong size for the fasteners the mechanic actually uses.

At this moment, many founders make a specific mistake. Instead of fixing the screwdriver, they start asking: "What else could this thing be used for? Maybe I can use it to cook? Maybe it could be a cleaning tool?"

Seibel's response is blunt:

> "If your screwdriver doesn't help the mechanic solve the problem, keep the mechanic, keep the problem. Fix the screwdriver! That's the thing that's broken."

The screwdriver is your solution. The mechanic is your customer. The problem — turning screws effectively — is the thing you are solving. When your solution fails, the correct response is not to shop the solution around to different customers or different problems. The correct response is to improve the solution until it works for the customer and the problem you committed to.

This is hard to do because it requires a kind of humility that founders rarely have in abundance. It means admitting that your first attempt was wrong. It means resisting the temptation to declare victory by redefining the problem to match what you happened to build. It means staying in the uncomfortable zone where your product is not yet good enough, and instead of escaping that discomfort through pivoting, you work through it.

Seibel's framework has an important corollary: continue improving on your solution until it actually solves the problem. Do not pivot to other problems. The iteration is not infinite — it has a clear success condition. The mechanic can turn screws effectively with your screwdriver. When that happens, you have earned the right to think about what comes next.

## The Pre-Launch Sequence

Stepping back, Seibel's four goals form a logical sequence that every pre-launch startup should follow:

1. **Launch** something minimal — get it out the door before you feel ready.
2. **Get initial customers** — do whatever it takes to put the product in front of real users and see if they can extract value.
3. **Talk to those users** — listen to what they say, and more importantly, watch what they do. Do not dismiss their feedback because the product is not "finished."
4. **Iterate on the solution** — when it does not work, fix it. Do not abandon the customer or the problem. Hold the screwdriver loosely and keep reshaping it until it fits.

The thread that runs through all four goals is a single principle: reduce the time between having an idea and discovering whether it is worth pursuing. Every day that passes without that discovery is a day of risk accumulation, not progress. Seibel's framework is an argument for compressing that timeline as aggressively as possible, because the alternative — building in secret, hoping you are right — is the most expensive way to be wrong.
