# How to Get and Test Startup Ideas

**Author:** Michael Seibel (former CEO of Y Combinator, co-founder of Justin.tv/Twitch and Socialcam)
**Date:** December 2023
**Source:** [YC Startup Library](https://www.ycombinator.com/library/7x-how-to-get-and-test-ideas) | [Startup Archive](https://www.startuparchive.org/p/michael-seibel-on-how-to-get-and-test-startup-ideas)

---

## The Misconception: Your Idea Doesn't Have to Be Great

"There's a common misconception that your idea has to be great in order to start a company, and the first thing I want to do is destroy that misconception."

Seibel speaks from experience. He was a co-founder of Justin.tv, which later became Twitch and sold to Amazon for nearly a billion dollars. The original idea? An online reality TV show — a person wearing a camera on their head 24/7, broadcasting their life. It's hard to draw a straight line from that to the live-streaming platform where millions watch gamers and chat with friends. But that's exactly what it became. The initial idea and the eventual outcome often bear little resemblance to each other.

The trap founders fall into is thinking they need to have the perfect idea from day one. They don't. What they need is the willingness to start somewhere and the discipline to find their way to something that works. The idea you begin with is a starting point, not a destination.

---

## Start With a Problem, Not an Idea

"Starting with ideas is tricky because people immediately want to grade your idea. It's a lot easier to start with a problem and think about how you grade a problem."

When you present an idea, people's instinct is to judge it — is it good? Is it bad? Will it work? That instinct is usually unhelpful. A much more productive framing is to start with a problem. Problems are easier to evaluate: How many people have this problem? How acute is the pain? How often does it occur? Is it getting worse over time? These are questions you can actually investigate, whereas grading an abstract idea tends to devolve into speculation.

When you think about a problem, your first filter should be personal connection. Ask yourself: Is this a problem I have? Is it a problem my friends or family have? Is it a problem that exists at work or in my community? Having a personal connection to a problem serves two essential functions. First, it gives you a built-in truth detector — you can tell whether a proposed solution is even in the ballpark because you understand the problem from the inside. Second, when you feel discouraged, and the first ten solutions you build don't work, your personal connection to the problem keeps you going long enough to find one that does.

Instead of keeping a little notebook of startup ideas, Seibel recommends keeping a problem book. Write down the problems you encounter in daily life. Note what your friends complain about, what your family struggles with, what frustrates you at work. Over time, this practice builds a rich inventory of real problems to draw from — far more useful than a list of half-baked product concepts.

### Brainstorm Problems With Potential Co-Founders

Another benefit of starting with problems rather than ideas is that problems make for better co-founder recruiting conversations. When you brainstorm with friends about problems, you can see how well you trade ideas, how effectively you build on each other's thinking, and whether you communicate well together. A good brainstorming session around a problem is one of the best signals of co-founder compatibility. If you can spend an hour going back and forth on a problem — elaborating on it, challenging assumptions, imagining approaches — and it feels productive and energizing, that's a strong indicator.

## Why Are You Uniquely Qualified?

Once you've found a problem worth pursuing, the next question to ask is: "Why am I uniquely qualified to work on this problem?"

By "qualified," Seibel doesn't mean in the traditional résumé sense — twenty-five years of industry experience or a PhD in the relevant domain. He means something more specific: Is there an angle on this problem that you understand but others don't? Is there a way of attacking it, a perspective on it, or a personal experience with it that you possess and that isn't generally reflected in existing solutions?

This question forces you to be honest about whether this problem is actually a good fit for you as a founder. If you have no edge — no unique insight, no uncommon perspective, no special access — then even a genuinely important problem may not be the right one for you to work on.

In this phase, it's often helpful to look at who has tried to solve this problem before. Study the products that have attempted to address it, especially the ones that failed. Understand what they thought their unique insight was, and ask yourself whether yours is genuinely different. This is your gut check: is this the right fit for me?

## Great Companies Have Secrets

Peter Thiel famously argues that "great companies have secrets: specific reasons for success that other people don't see." The unique qualification question is essentially a search for your secret. What do you believe about this problem that most people — including other smart, capable people — don't believe yet? What truth about the world, the market, or the user have you seen that others are still blind to?

This doesn't have to be a world-historical secret. It can be as simple as: "I've noticed that when this type of user encounters this problem, the existing tools actually make it worse because they were designed for a different kind of user." That insight, if true and important, is a secret — and it gives you a thesis to test.

## Build an MVP — Quickly, Within a Week

Once you've identified a problem and have some conviction about your unique angle, the next step is to think about your MVP: your minimum viable product. What is the very first solution you're going to build and put in front of users to test whether you can actually help them solve this problem?

The most important thing about your MVP is speed. Seibel emphasizes that an MVP can often be built and released in days or weeks, not months. It won't be perfect. It might not even be something you're proud of. But it can be the beginning of learning whether the problem is solvable at all — let alone whether you can solve it.

The bar for an MVP is not quality; it's whether it lets you learn something real. If you spend six months polishing something before anyone sees it, you've spent six months learning nothing about whether you're building the right thing.

## Fall in Love With the Problem and the Customer, Not the Product

"A lot of people fall in love with their product and are not in love with their problem or their customer. I advise the opposite. Be in love with your problem. Be in love with your customer. And treat your product in a way that can change, develop, and improve."

This is perhaps the single most important mindset shift Seibel recommends. Founders who fall in love with their product become defensive when it doesn't work. They blame the users, the market, or the timing. Founders who fall in love with the problem treat the product as an instrument — one that can and should be rewritten, rebuilt, or entirely discarded if it fails to solve the problem.

Your MVP is most likely not going to solve the problem. It's probably going to solve it poorly, if at all. That's not a failure; it's the expected first step in a learning process. The question isn't whether your first product works. The question is whether you learn enough from it to make the next one better.

The fastest way to avoid falling in love with your product is to release it quickly. When you've only invested a week in building something, you're much more willing to hear that it's wrong and start over. When you've invested six months, sunk-cost psychology takes over and you start rationalizing away negative feedback.

## Handpick Your Initial Users

Once you have an MVP, be extremely deliberate about who your first users are. "The best startups very heavily filter the people who are able to use the initial product and make sure that they're the right type of initial customer."

Your initial users should almost be handpicked. You should have a strong opinion about who your initial customer is — and you shouldn't waste time trying to solve the problem for someone who isn't willing to try a startup's imperfect early product, or for whom the problem isn't genuinely important in their life.

The purpose of an MVP is not to see how many people want to use your product. It's not a growth experiment. The purpose is to see whether, for any specific group of people, your solution actually solves the problem you set out to solve. A small number of deeply engaged, carefully chosen users will teach you far more than a large number of casual, poorly matched ones.

This means filtering aggressively. If you're building something for software engineers, don't give it to marketers and ask what they think. If you're building something for small retail businesses, don't test it on enterprise procurement teams. Find the people who genuinely have the problem, who feel the pain acutely, and who are motivated enough to try something rough in exchange for relief.

## The Goal: Learn Whether the Problem Is Solvable

Bringing it all together, Seibel's framework is a learning engine disguised as a product development process:

1. **Start with a problem** you have a personal connection to — one you understand deeply enough to evaluate solutions honestly.
2. **Interrogate your unique qualification** — why are you, specifically, the right person to tackle this? What do you see that others don't?
3. **Build an MVP in days or weeks**, not months. It doesn't need to be good; it needs to exist so you can learn from it.
4. **Handpick a small set of initial users** who genuinely have the problem and will give you honest feedback.
5. **Watch whether it actually solves the problem for them.** If it doesn't, change the product. If no version of the product solves it, question whether the problem is solvable at all — or whether you've understood it correctly.

At every stage, the focus stays on the problem and the customer. The product is just the current hypothesis. The faster you can test hypotheses and discard the ones that fail, the faster you'll converge on something that works. That's how Justin.tv — an online reality TV show — became Twitch, a platform worth nearly a billion dollars. The founders stayed focused on the problem (people want to share live experiences and connect around them) and let the product evolve until it found its form.
