# One Order of Operations for Starting a Startup

**Author:** Michael Seibel
**Source:** https://www.ycombinator.com/blog/one-order-of-operations-for-starting-a-startup
**Date:** March 20, 2018

---

## Introduction: Why the "Great Idea" Theory is Wrong

More often than not, when a talented technical person thinks about becoming a founder, their number one blocker is that they don't have an idea. At some point during their formative years they learned that every great startup started with a great idea, and if the idea isn't amazing — usually as judged by peers, parents, or other people with little startup experience — the startup will fail.

This is a myth. Michael Seibel's first startup idea (really Justin Kan's idea) was to create a live reality TV show where people would walk around with cameras on their heads, livestreaming their lives 24/7. That company eventually became Twitch.tv and sold to Amazon for almost a billion dollars. Airbnb started with the idea that when you want to attend a conference, sometimes all the hotels in that city are sold out — so wouldn't it be great to have a site where you can find an airbed to crash on? In 2017, over 200 million nights were booked on Airbnb, almost none of them fitting the original purpose of the site.

Seibel offers an alternative to the conventional "great idea" theory: a sequential order of operations that can take a first-time founder from nothing to a launched minimum viable product (MVP). This is not the only path, and it may not be the best path, but it is a path that has worked for a number of YC companies. The key insight is that **each step builds on the one before it. Skipping steps is the most common cause of early-stage failure.**

---

## Step 1: Find a Problem, Not an Idea

Most aspiring founders start with an idea and then go looking for a problem it might solve. Seibel inverts this: start with a problem you care about.

Is there any particular problem you are passionate about? For younger founders, that problem can come from your experience growing up, from your family and friends, your personal interests, or from school. For older founders it could also come from experiences at work, being married, or being someone's partner.

For the Airbnb founders, their original problem was how to pay rent. For Justin.tv/Twitch, the original problem Justin and Emmett had was "what startup should we work on next." Some problems seem important and impactful at the beginning; others feel like trivial toys. Seibel has seen both types grow into billion-dollar companies. The important thing is that the problem is real to you — something you have personally experienced or observed closely.

**Why this matters:** When you start with a problem rather than an idea, you are tethered to reality. An idea can be seductive in the abstract but collapse on contact with actual users. A problem you've lived gives you genuine insight into what a solution needs to do. It also gives you the motivation to persist through the inevitable difficult stretches, because you care about solving it whether or not the first attempt works.

---

## Step 2: Brainstorm Solutions with Smart Friends (and Potential Co-Founders)

Once you've settled on a problem, find some friends and brainstorm potential solutions. Those friends can be other people who also have the same problem, but they don't have to be. They can be people you went to school with, people from work, or people you've met in your personal life.

What's most important is that you pick friends who are **smart and fun to brainstorm with**. When someone is fun to brainstorm with, instead of rejecting potential solutions as they are said aloud, they riff on them and improve upon them. If you're not technical, Seibel encourages you to brainstorm with friends or colleagues who are.

A successful brainstorm session does two things at once:

1. **It produces a solution you can get excited about.** The creative friction of a good brainstorm turns an identified problem into a specific, actionable approach. This is the spark that can make your startup come alive.

2. **It surfaces potential co-founders.** Because the people you brainstormed with feel some ownership for the solution you came up with together, this is the perfect time to ask them to work with you to actually build it. Pick your brainstorming partners carefully — they may become your company.

If you're not technical and don't have access to technical people, Seibel's advice is to go work at a startup and make those friends. At the end of the day, having the right founding team is such a fast track to building a successful startup that it's worth investing a year or two building those relationships before you start.

---

## Step 3: Validate the Problem is Real — Talk to Potential Users Before Building

Once you have a problem and a rough sense of a solution, the next step is to verify that the problem actually exists for other people. This is where most founders go wrong: they build first and ask questions later.

Talk to people who you believe have the problem. Ask them about their experience. Listen more than you talk. The goal is not to pitch your solution but to understand whether the problem is something they actually feel, how severe it is, and what they currently do to work around it. If people aren't actively trying to solve the problem with some kind of workaround — however clumsy — it may not be a real problem.

**The litmus test:** Do people's eyes light up when you describe the problem? Do they immediately start telling you stories about how it affects them? Or do they nod politely and change the subject? The former is evidence of a real problem; the latter is evidence that you may need to find a different one.

---

## Step 4: Build an MVP as Quickly as Possible — Target Within One Week

At this point, the most important thing is to turn the spark into a fire by building and launching a minimum viable product. Seibel defines an MVP as **the smallest possible product you can build that allows you to start offering your service to users.**

Often you need very little software to start. Resist the temptation to build your complete solution, because in reality you have no idea whether it will work. It is better to build an extremely stripped-down version of your solution and start seeing if users actually want to use it.

Airbnb's first version had almost no features — no map view, no profiles, no messaging, no payments — and the founders referred to it as "Airbnb Lite." They built it in under a month. It is totally okay if you need to do a bunch of manual work that doesn't scale on the back end. If users actually want what you are making, you'll have time to fix everything and make it scale later.

**Once you have your first users of any kind, you have a startup.** But you still need someone on the team with the technical skills to ship the MVP. This is why Step 2 (finding technical co-founders) comes before this step.

The one-week target is aspirational but important as a forcing function. It prevents you from adding features that feel essential but aren't. It forces you to ask: "What is the absolute core of what we're building?" Everything else can wait.

---

## Step 5: Launch Immediately — Even If It's Bad

Seibel is emphatic: launch your MVP as soon as it exists. Do not wait until it is polished. Do not wait until you are proud of it. Do not wait until it has the features your friends told you were "must-haves." Launch it in whatever state it is in and get it in front of real users.

The reasons founders delay launch are familiar and almost always wrong:

- "It's not ready yet." It will never feel ready.
- "What if people hate it?" Better to find out now than after six more months of building.
- "We need feature X first." You almost certainly don't.
- "We're embarrassed by how it looks." Your early users care about whether it solves their problem, not whether it's beautiful.

Launching is the transition from theory to practice. Until you launch, everything you believe about your product is a hypothesis. Launching is the first real test. It also creates an irreversible commitment: now there are users who depend on you, which is the most powerful motivator to keep building.

---

## Step 6: Get Initial Users by Any Means Necessary

Your first users will not find you. You must find them. Seibel recommends doing whatever it takes to get those first few people using your product — manual recruitment, hand-picking, direct outreach, personal networks, anything that works.

At YC, this is often phrased as "do things that don't scale." Airbnb's founders went door to door in New York, taking professional photos of their hosts' apartments. Stripe's founders would literally set up Stripe accounts for potential users on the spot, watching them type in their details. None of this is scalable, and that's exactly the point. At this stage, you are not trying to build a growth machine. You are trying to get a small number of real users so you can learn from them.

The bar for "any means necessary" is low: email people you know, post in relevant forums, reach out to strangers on social media, attend meetups, offer to set people up manually. If you can't convince a dozen people to try your product through sheer force of will, you may need to reconsider whether the problem is urgent enough.

---

## Step 7: Talk to Those Users Extensively — Learn What They Actually Need

Once you have users, your primary job is to talk to them. Not survey them, not A/B test on them — actually have conversations with them. Seibel's guidance is to hold the problem and the customer tight, and hold the solution loose.

Your early users are a goldmine of information about what you should build next. Ask them:

- What problem were they trying to solve when they found your product?
- What did they use before?
- What's the most frustrating part of using your product?
- What would make them stop using it?
- What do they wish it did that it doesn't?

The most important signals are often not what users say they want, but what their behavior reveals. If they are using your product in unexpected ways, pay attention — that may be the real product. If they are abandoning it at a particular step, that step is broken regardless of what they tell you in conversation.

Seibel emphasizes extensive conversation because a few surface-level data points can be deeply misleading. One user's offhand feature request can send you building for weeks in the wrong direction. But when you hear the same thing from multiple users across multiple conversations, a pattern emerges. That pattern is worth acting on.

---

## Step 8: Don't Fall in Love with Your Product; Fall in Love with the Problem

This is one of Seibel's most important principles. Founders who fall in love with their product become defensive when users don't like it. They blame the users instead of the product. They resist changing course because they are emotionally invested in code they've already written. This is fatal.

Falling in love with the problem is different. If you love the problem, you are open to any solution that solves it. You are willing to throw away months of work if a better approach emerges. You listen to user feedback not as criticism of your work but as clues about how to solve the problem better.

Twitch is a perfect example: Justin.tv started as a general lifecasting platform. The founders noticed that a subset of users were streaming themselves playing video games, and those streams were getting disproportionate engagement. Because they loved the problem — building a platform for live streaming — and not the specific product of lifecasting, they were able to pivot. If they had been in love with the lifecasting concept, they would have dismissed the gaming use case as a niche tangent instead of recognizing it as the real business.

**Practical rule:** When user feedback contradicts your vision, the user is usually right about the problem and usually wrong about the solution. Your job is to extract the problem insight while remaining humble about your proposed solution.

---

## Step 9: Focus on Making a Few Users Really Happy, Not Many Users Semi-Happy

At this stage, the goal is not growth. The goal is depth. A handful of users who love your product are worth infinitely more than a large number of users who are merely indifferent.

A user who loves your product will:

- Tell other people about it (word of mouth, the most powerful acquisition channel)
- Forgive bugs and missing features
- Give you detailed, thoughtful feedback
- Stick around through rough patches
- Become the foundation of a community

A user who is semi-happy will churn at the first friction point and tell no one.

Seibel's advice aligns with Paul Graham's famous maxim: "It's better to have 100 users who love you than 10,000 users who sort of like you." The 100 users who love you form a base from which you can grow. The 10,000 indifferent users give you a vanity metric and nothing more.

Operationally, this means: when you have a choice between building a feature that will make your existing power users ecstatic and building a feature that might attract new users, choose the former. When a user reports a bug or requests something, prioritize the users who are most engaged. Your most passionate users are your best product managers.

---

## Step 10: Only After Validation Should You Think About Scaling, Hiring, or Fundraising

This is the step that founders most want to skip. Once you have a working product and a few users, the temptation is to immediately raise money, hire a team, and scale. Seibel warns that this is premature.

The sequence matters because each step validates assumptions the later steps depend on:

- **Scaling** assumes you have built something people want. If you scale before validating that, you are scaling a broken product.
- **Hiring** assumes you know what roles you need. Before you understand your users, you don't know whether you need another engineer, a designer, a salesperson, or a customer support person.
- **Fundraising** assumes you can tell a compelling story backed by evidence. Investors invest in execution, not ideas. A launched product with real users who love it is the strongest possible fundraising pitch.

Seibel identifies two common failure paths that violate this sequence:

**Failure Path 1: Come up with an idea and then pitch investors.** Founders who do this think that investors, by investing, will validate their idea — and that if they can't raise money, they don't have a good idea. In reality, good investors are interested in teams who are actually executing their idea, and they tend to shy away from teams that are merely pitching.

**Failure Path 2: Outsource the build.** Founders who can't write code (and whose co-founders can't either) often want to pay someone else to build their product, then launch, raise money, and build out a tech team. The problem is that good investors are investing in the team. If you're building a tech startup and no one on your team can do the technical work, the bar to raise investment is much higher — Seibel estimates you need 10x the traction to get a good investor to overlook a non-technical team. Moreover, outsourced engineers aren't as motivated as you are and are often expensive.

---

## The Sequence is the Whole Point

The most important idea in Seibel's framework is that **the order matters.** Each step builds on the previous one, and skipping steps creates fragility. You cannot validate a problem without talking to users (Step 3). You cannot get meaningful user feedback without launching something (Step 5). You cannot raise money effectively without evidence of traction (Step 10).

The path from "I want to start a startup but don't have an idea" to "I have a launched product with users who love it" does not require genius. It requires following a sequence of concrete, achievable steps. Most people who fail at starting a startup don't fail because their idea was bad. They fail because they tried to start at Step 8 (fundraising) or Step 9 (scaling) without doing Steps 1 through 7.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Problems, not ideas.** Start with something you've personally experienced.
2. **Brainstorm with the right people.** Smart, constructive friends who build on ideas rather than tear them down — and who may become co-founders.
3. **Validate before building.** Talk to potential users; listen more than you talk.
4. **Build the smallest thing that works.** An MVP should take a week, not months.
5. **Launch immediately.** Embarrassment is temporary; learning is permanent.
6. **Recruit users manually.** Do things that don't scale. Get to a dozen real users by any means necessary.
7. **Talk to your users constantly.** Hold the problem tight; hold the solution loose.
8. **Love the problem, not your product.** Be willing to throw away code when a better solution emerges.
9. **Depth over breadth.** Make a few users really happy. Ignore vanity metrics.
10. **Validate first, scale later.** Fundraising, hiring, and growth come after you've built something people want.

Building a successful startup is extremely hard. When you choose to skip steps, your road becomes much harder and less likely to lead to success. As Seibel puts it: why make things more painful on yourself?
