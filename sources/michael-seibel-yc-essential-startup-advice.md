# YC's Essential Startup Advice

**Source:** https://www.michaelseibel.com/blog/yc-s-essential-startup-advice  
**Authors:** Geoff Ralston and Michael Seibel  
**Originally published on:** Y Combinator Blog

---

A lot of the advice Y Combinator gives startups is tactical — meant to be helpful on a day-to-day or week-to-week basis. But some advice is more fundamental. Collected here is what YC considers the most important, most transformative guidance for startups. Whether common sense or counter-intuitive, the principles below will help most startups find their path to success.

---

## Launch Now

The first thing YC always tells founders is to **launch their product right away**. This is the only way to fully understand customers' problems and whether the product meets their needs. Launching a mediocre product as soon as possible, and then talking to customers and iterating, is much better than waiting to build the "perfect" product.

This holds as long as the product contains a **"quantum of utility"** — enough genuine value for customers that it overwhelms whatever problems any rough edges might present. The market teaches you what to build; your imagination alone cannot substitute for real usage.

Too many founders delay launch in pursuit of polish, completeness, or feature parity. But until users are actually interacting with what you've built, you are operating on assumptions. Launching forces those assumptions to collide with reality.

---

## Do Things That Don't Scale

Once launched, founders should deliberately **do things that don't scale**. Many startup advisors push companies to scale way too early, requiring the building of technology and processes to support that scaling. When premature, this is a waste of time and effort — and often leads to failure or even startup death.

Instead, YC tells startups to **get their first customer by any means necessary**, even by manual work that couldn't be managed for more than ten customers, much less a hundred or a thousand. At this stage, founders are still trying to figure out what needs to be built, and the best way to do that is to talk directly to customers.

The canonical example is Airbnb. The founders originally offered to "professionally" photograph the homes and apartments of their earliest customers to make listings more attractive to renters. Then they went and took the photographs themselves. The listings improved, conversions improved, and they had amazing conversations with their customers. This was entirely unscalable — yet proved essential in learning how to build a vibrant marketplace.

---

## Find the 90/10 Solution

Talking to users usually yields a long, complicated list of features to build. YC partner Paul Buchheit (PB) always advises founders in this situation to **look for the "90/10 solution"**: a way to accomplish 90% of what you want with only 10% of the work, effort, and time.

If you search hard for it, there is almost always a 90/10 solution available. Most importantly, a 90% solution to a real customer problem that is available right away is much better than a 100% solution that takes ages to build. Speed of delivery compounds: getting something in front of users today generates learning that a perfect launch six months from now can never recover.

---

## Write Code and Talk to Users — Avoid Distractions

As companies begin to grow, there are often tons of potential distractions: conferences, dinners, meetings with venture capitalists or large company corporate development types, chasing after press coverage, and so on. YC co-founder Jessica Livingston created a comprehensive list of the wrong things on which to focus.

The most important tasks for an early-stage company are deceptively simple: **write code and talk to users**. For any company, software or otherwise, this means: launch something, talk to your users to see if it serves their needs, and then take their feedback and iterate. These tasks should occupy almost all of your time and focus. For great companies, this cycle never ends.

Sam Altman calls everything else **"fake work"** — activities that feel productive but don't actually move the company forward. Fake work tends to be more fun than real work, which is precisely why it's dangerous. Founders gravitate toward it because real work — writing code, making sales calls, hearing that your product isn't good enough — is hard.

Similarly, as your company evolves there will be many times when founders must choose between multiple directions. It is nearly always better to **take the more ambitious path**.

---

## Choose Your Customers — And Fire Some

Most founders don't realize that they get to choose customers as much as customers get to choose them. **A small group of customers who love you is better than a large group who kind of like you.** Recruiting ten customers who have a burning problem is much more valuable than attracting a thousand customers who have a passing annoyance.

Sometimes it's critical for startups to **fire their customers**. Some customers cost way more than they provide in either revenue or learning. Justin.tv/Twitch only became a breakout success when they focused their efforts toward video game broadcasters and away from people trying to stream copyrighted content. The customers who love you shape your trajectory; the wrong customers pull you off course.

---

## Growth Follows Product-Market Fit

Growth is always a focus for startups, since a startup without growth is usually a failure. However, how and when to grow is often misunderstood. YC pushes companies to talk to their users, build what they want, and iterate quickly — **growth is a natural result of doing these three things successfully**, not a goal to pursue independently.

Growth is not always the right choice. If you have not yet made something your customers want — in other words, have not found product-market fit — it makes little sense to grow. Poor retention is always the result. If you have an unprofitable product, growth merely drains cash from the company. As PB likes to say: it never makes sense to take 80 cents from a customer and then hand them a dollar back. **Unit economics really matter** — a basic fact that too many startups forget.

---

## Do Less, Really Well

Startup founders' intuition will always be to do more, whereas the best strategy is almost always to **do less, really well**. Founders are frequently tempted to chase big deals with large companies that represent amazing, company-validating relationships. However, deals between large companies and tiny startups seldom end well for the startup. They take too long, cost too much, and often fail completely.

One of the hardest things about doing a startup is choosing what to do, since you will always have an infinite list of things that could be done. It is vital that very early a startup choose **one or two key metrics** to measure success, then founders should choose what to do based nearly exclusively on how each task will impact those metrics.

When your early-stage product isn't working, it's tempting to immediately build new features to solve every problem the customer seems to have. Instead, talk to the customer and focus only on the most acute problem they have.

---

## All Startups Are Broken — That's Normal

Founders are often surprised to hear that they shouldn't worry if their company seems badly broken. **Nearly every startup has deep, fundamental issues**, even those that will end up being billion-dollar companies. Success is not determined by whether you are broken at the beginning, but rather what the founders do about the inevitable problems. Your job as a founder will often seem to be **continuously righting a capsized ship**. This is normal.

---

## Ignore Your Competitors

It is very difficult as a new startup founder not to obsess about competition, actual and potential. But spending any time worrying about your competitors is nearly always a very bad idea. **Startup companies always die of suicide, not murder.** There will come a time when competitive dynamics are intensely important to the success or failure of your company, but it is highly unlikely to be true in the first year or two. The thing most likely to kill your startup is your own decisions — not a competitor.

---

## Fundraising: Raise Fast and Get Back to Work

The first, best bit of fundraising advice is to **raise money as quickly as possible and then get back to work**. It is often easy to see when a company is fundraising by looking at their growth curve — when it flattens out, they are raising money.

Equally important: **valuation is not equal to success or even probability of success**. Some of Y Combinator's very best companies raised on tiny initial valuations. Airbnb, Dropbox, and Twitch are all good examples. Fundraising rounds are not milestones; they are financing events.

Finally, it is vital to remember that **the money you raise is not your money**. You have a fiduciary and ethical duty to spend the money only to improve the prospects of your company.

---

## Stay Sane, Be Nice, and Take Care of Founder Relationships

During the inevitable craziness of startup life, founders must take breaks, spend time with friends and family, get enough sleep, and exercise — in between bouts of extraordinarily intense, focused work.

Most companies fail fast because **founders fall out**. The relationships with your cofounders matter more than you think, and open, honest communications between founders makes future debacles much less likely. In fact, one of the best things you can do to make your startup successful — and to be successful in life — is to **simply be nice**.

---

## The Pocket Guide of Essential YC Advice

- Launch now
- Build something people want
- Do things that don't scale
- Find the 90/10 solution
- Find 10–100 customers who love your product
- All startups are badly broken at some point
- Write code — talk to users
- "It's not your money"
- Growth is the result of a great product, not the precursor
- Don't scale your team/product until you have built something people want
- Valuation is not equal to success or even probability of success
- Avoid long negotiated deals with big customers if you can
- Avoid big company corporate development queries — they will only waste time
- Avoid conferences unless they are the best way to get customers
- Pre-product-market fit — do things that don't scale: remain small and nimble
- Startups can only solve one problem well at any given time
- Founder relationships matter more than you think
- Sometimes you need to fire your customers (they might be killing you)
- Ignore your competitors — you will more likely die of suicide than murder
- Most companies don't die because they run out of money
- Be nice! Or at least don't be a jerk
- Get sleep and exercise — take care of yourself
