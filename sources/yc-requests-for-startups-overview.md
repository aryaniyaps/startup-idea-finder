## What Is Requests for Startups?

Requests for Startups (RFS) is a YC tradition where partners publicly share startup ideas they'd like to see founders tackle. Each RFS is a signal from someone who sees thousands of startups each year — they've developed conviction that a particular problem is both important and newly solvable.

An RFS is not a filter or a requirement. YC is explicit: **"If one excites you, take it as extra validation to dive in, but you don't need to work on these ideas to apply to YC."** The RFS program simply reveals where partners' thinking is concentrated right now.

The RFS tradition traces back to Paul Graham's early essays, where he would articulate ideas he wished founders would pursue. Over the years, many successful YC companies emerged from concepts that started as RFS entries — it's a proven pipeline for surfacing overlooked opportunities.

---

## How RFS Works as an Idea Signal

Each RFS is written by a YC partner (and occasionally a YC founder) who has deep exposure to a domain. The format is consistent:

1. **The current state** — What's broken, expensive, or stuck
2. **The shift** — What changed to make this solvable *now*
3. **The opportunity** — What the winning company looks like
4. **The call to action** — "If you're building this, we want to talk"

The structural insight: **RFS entries are not wish lists — they're theses about timing.** Each one argues that a specific enabling condition (a technology, a cost curve, a regulatory shift) has flipped, creating a window that wasn't open before. Understanding *why now* is the most valuable part of reading them.

## Summer 2026 RFS: "AI Has Stopped Being a Feature"

The Summer 2026 batch is overwhelmingly AI-focused, but not in the shallow sense. YC's framing: **"AI has stopped being a feature and started being the foundation."** They're excited about startups rebuilding software, services, and silicon, *and* pushing AI into the physical world. Several RFS entries come directly from YC founders sharing what they're seeing on the frontier.

## The Summer 2026 RFS Entries

### 1. AI for Low-Pesticide Agriculture

**By:** Garry Tan (CEO & President, Y Combinator)  
**Video:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=uwe-5PB9p4Y

**The current state:** Modern agriculture runs on chemicals — pesticides, herbicides, fungicides. Pesticide residues are everywhere: in food, water, and soil. Glyphosate health concerns are growing globally. Meanwhile, weeds and pests evolve resistance; farmers spray more for diminishing returns. Costs go up, margins go down, and the pipeline for new chemicals is slower and more expensive than ever. Farmers are stuck in a loop: use more chemicals → get diminishing results → pay more → take on more risk. But they can't simply stop — if pests win, crops die. It's existential.

**The shift:** Three things changed simultaneously:
- AI can now identify individual weeds and pests in real time
- Sensors and cameras got cheap enough to deploy everywhere
- Robotics can act with precision — treating one plant instead of blanketing an entire field
- Biology is catching up: microbes, peptides, and RNA-based solutions can replace entire classes of synthetic chemicals; we can engineer plants to defend themselves

**The opportunity:** Agriculture is one of the world's largest markets. A company that cuts pesticide use by 90% *and* helps farmers grow more food isn't just a good business — it's a generational company. Adoption won't be slow when you simultaneously lower costs and increase yields.

### 2. AI-Native Discovery Engines

**By:** Jon Xu (General Partner, Y Combinator)  
**Video:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mcXrvjPKJas

**The current state:** Scientific discovery has run on the same loop for centuries: hypothesize → experiment → interpret → repeat. The loop works but is slow — every step requires significant human effort.

**The shift:** Frontier models have reached PhD-level performance on many scientific reasoning benchmarks. Models can now propose hypotheses, generate experiments, analyze data, and suggest next steps. The frontier is shifting from copilot research assistants to intelligent systems that run *closed* discovery loops. This is already happening in drug discovery, materials science, and protein engineering — models propose candidate molecules, automated labs synthesize and test them, and results feed back to iteratively improve candidates.

**The opportunity:** Companies that make meaningful contributions to scientific progress won't just sell research copilots. They'll be AI-native discovery engines that work alongside researchers to propose and validate hypotheses in a continuous closed loop.

### 3. AI-Native Service Companies

**By:** Gustaf Alströmer (Group Partner, Y Combinator)  
**Video:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=lsRnwM_ZdJY

**The current state:** AI models can now do complex work far beyond engineering. Historically, services became SaaS software. More recently (2023–2025), they became AI copilots — tools that help people do their jobs.

**The shift:** The next step: AI-native companies that don't sell software — they *sell the service*. Instead of giving you a tool, they just do the work. The total spend on services is many times larger than the spend on software. And many of these services are already outsourced, making them easier to replace with an AI-native product.

**Areas of particular interest:**
- Insurance brokerage
- Accounting, tax, and audit
- Compliance
- Healthcare administration

**The opportunity:** Build a company that replaces a service — not just improves it.

### 4. AI Personalized Medicine

**By:** Ankit Gupta (Group Partner, Y Combinator)  
**Video:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fzVuQ2sYEn8

**The current state:** Intelligent agents are enabling a new level of personalization in medical care. We can now use agent harnesses like Claude Code to analyze personalized health data — diagnostic tests, genome scans, EHR data, wearables — to get highly accurate, user-specific suggestions.

**The shift — two concurrent revolutions:**
1. The cost of generating personalized diagnostics is plummeting. Genome sequencing costs have fallen faster than Moore's Law. New diagnostics are entering the market enabling early detection of diverse health signals.
2. The cost of printing n-of-1 genetic therapies is plummeting. We can now design and deliver personalized medicines through delivery vectors like mRNA. The FDA has expressed more openness to patients trying these procedures.

**The opportunity:** Abundant data and intelligence can help patients more accurately assess disease risk and democratize access to treatments for the most serious illnesses. A wide variety of new startups will support every step of this ecosystem — personalized diagnostics, delivery, and care coordination.

### 5. Company Brain

**By:** Tom Blomfield (Group Partner, Y Combinator)  
**Video:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IaWIazkWWog

**The current state:** The biggest blocker to AI automation of companies is no longer the models — they got so good so quickly. The blocker is now *domain knowledge*. Every company has critical know-how scattered across people's heads, old email accounts, Slack threads, support tickets, and databases. The company works because humans vaguely remember where that knowledge lives and how to apply it. AI agents can't operate like that.

**The shift:** If we want every company to run on AI automation, we need a new primitive: **a company brain**. This is not company-wide search or a chatbot over documents. It's a *living map* of how a company works — how refunds get handled, how pricing exceptions are decided, how engineers respond to incidents. The company brain extracts knowledge from fragmented sources, structures it, keeps it current, and turns it into an executable skills file for AI systems to do the work safely and consistently.

**The opportunity:** The company brain becomes the missing layer between raw company data and reliable AI automation. Every company in the world is going to need one.

### 6. Counter-Swarm Defense

**By:** Tyler Bosmeny (Group Partner, Y Combinator)  
**Video:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=O8JiNHcI3jI

**The current state:** A swarm of cheap Iranian drones recently took out an AWS data center. Nobody stopped them. While everyone races to stop *individual* drones, the next wave is worse: swarms — cheap, autonomous, jam-resistant, and lethal. A Patriot missile costs $3 million. An FPV drone costs $500. All of the cost advantage lies with the attackers.

Today's counter-drone defense is a messy pile of radars, cameras, jammers, interceptors, humans with binoculars — systems that don't talk to each other. That might work against a hobby drone. It won't work against a swarm.

**The shift:** Drone defense is looking less like operating a weapon and more like running a real-time distributed system. The winning companies will look more like Cloudflare than Raytheon.

**Opportunity areas:**
- High-capacity interceptors — a single platform that neutralizes 50 drones, not one
- Software that fuses every sensor and defender into a single real-time picture
- Non-kinetic defenses: aerosols that foul rotors, streamers that entangle swarms
- Attacks on the autonomy stack itself (radio jamming is becoming obsolete)

### 7. Dynamic Software Interfaces

**By:** Ankit Gupta (Group Partner, Y Combinator)  
**Video:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_2LcG-3zCdY

**The current state:** Before AI, all users of a piece of software interacted with the same interface — only light customizations like themes or view options. Most software has a one-size-fits-all feel. The exception is enterprise software, where forward-deployed engineers customize software per customer.

**The shift:** Coding agents have now gotten good enough to allow users to become their own forward-deployed engineers, radically customizing the software they consume. Imagine an email client where one user's interface looks like a task list and another's looks like an events calendar — but both share underlying primitives and design decisions the software team built and shipped.

**The opportunity:** Software companies will ship shared primitives with the full intention that users heavily modify the final interfaces. This requires rethinking the entire stack of software delivery:
- How does a developer make software accessible to the user's coding agents?
- Do they deliver source code rather than packaged binaries?
- Can users modify middleware on the fly, not just front-end elements?

### 8. Electronics in Space

**By:** Philip Johnston (Founder, Starcloud — YC portfolio)  
**Video:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pAV8f4Snx7Q

**The current state:** We're about to see an enormous increase in humanity's capacity to put things in space because of reusable rockets from SpaceX and Stoke Space.

**The shift:** This means enormous amounts of new compute capacity will be needed in space. The particular electronics needed: **inference chips optimized for space** — slightly optimized for mass, thermal, and radiation. There will be an absolutely enormous market for these chips. Johnston specifically calls out talent at SpaceX and NVIDIA who have chip design experience.

**The opportunity:** Build the inference chips that will power the next generation of space-based compute infrastructure.

### 9. Hardware Supply Chain

**By:** Nicolas Dessaigne (Group Partner, Y Combinator)  
**Video:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0gCeJFyRYx4

**The current state:** YC is funding more hardware companies than ever — medical devices, home robots, space companies. But building hardware in the US is still far too slow compared to China. In Shenzhen, a team can go from design to a new physical part in a day. In the US, that same loop often takes *weeks*. The gap compounds.

**The problem isn't just the supply chain — it's iteration speed.** China wins because hardware teams can move fast: dense supplier networks, rapid turnaround, tight coordination between design and production. In the US, this system barely exists. A few startups are building pieces (Hlabs for actuators, Prototyping.io for rapid mechanical parts), but the overall stack is still missing.

**The opportunity:** Startups that:
- Produce parts dramatically faster
- Enable rapid hardware iteration
- Tightly integrate design, manufacturing, and logistics
Help hardware teams move at an order of magnitude faster.

### 10. Industrial Capabilities in Space

**By:** Adi Oltean (Founder, Starcloud — YC portfolio)  
**Video:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=S8Z2v2bxocM

**The current state:** The broad idea: developing industrial capabilities on the moon and in space.

**The opportunity:** Extracting raw materials — silicon, aluminum, iron, titanium — through electrolysis from lunar regolith. 3D printing complex structures from molten regolith on the moon, which should be more efficient than on Earth due to the lack of supports (lower gravity means no need for support structures in additive manufacturing).

### 11. Inference Chips for Agent Workflows

**By:** Diana Hu (Group Partner, Y Combinator)  
**Video:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4YzFuHk84K0

**The current state:** Most AI chips are designed for a world where inference means "prompt in, response out." Agents don't work that way. They loop: calling tools, branching, backtracking, holding context across dozens of steps. That's a completely different hardware problem.

Current GPUs hit 30–40% of peak utilization on these workloads because the work is bursty — bouncing between memory-bound model calls, I/O-bound tool use, and CPU-bound orchestration. That gap is where purpose-built silicon wins.

**The shift:** NVIDIA bought Groq for $20 billion because it saw this coming. Google built TPU v7 for inference specifically. But nobody is designing for the *agent loop itself*: fast context switching between models, native speculative decoding, memory built for KV caches that persist across an entire execution graph.

**Key insight:** Groq's real insight wasn't the chip — it was the compiler that made the chip work. The same will be true for whoever builds this next. If you understand both chip architecture and how agents actually execute, this is a rare moment where both halves of that experience matter.

**The opportunity:** Purpose-built inference silicon for agentic AI — not prompt/response, but agent loops.

### 12. SaaS Challengers

**By:** Jared Friedman (Group Partner, Y Combinator)  
**Video:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DhYJ1GENLoQ

**The current state:** "Everyone's talking about how AI coding means the end of SaaS. Investors have wiped trillions off software market caps." If the incumbents really are this vulnerable, it should be the biggest startup opportunity in a decade.

**The shift:** The SaaS model won because custom software was too expensive — a five-person startup couldn't outbuild Salesforce. But AI has collapsed the cost of producing software by 10–100x. The moat that once protected legacy SaaS — millions of lines of code, built over decades — is gone.

**The opportunity spectrum:**
- Simplest: Clone an existing product and sell it for 1/10th the price
- Bolder: Build AI-native from the ground up — not a chatbot bolted onto a 2010 UI, but software that fundamentally rethinks the workflow
- Broader: Take 10 SaaS point solutions and bundle them into a single suite
- Open: Build an open-source replacement for a $50K-per-seat product, monetize through services and hosting

**Where to aim:** Not project management tools. Go after products that seem invulnerable: chip design software, ERPs, industrial control systems, supply chain management — the giant 10-million-line codebases that have been untouchable for decades.

**The framing:** "The last generation of great software companies was built by replacing on-premise with cloud. The next generation will be built by replacing legacy SaaS with AI-native software."

### 13. Software for Agents

**By:** Aaron Epstein (Group Partner, Y Combinator)  
**Video:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=515J0QD9p6w

**The current state:** "The next trillion users on the internet won't be people — they'll be AI agents." Agents are already browsing the web, doing research, making purchases, and managing legacy CRMs — but they're doing it on software designed for humans clicking buttons, which is slow, inconsistent, and brittle.

**The shift:** Agents need a completely different foundation. Instead of visual interfaces (forms, buttons, dashboards), they need machine-readable interfaces: **APIs, MCPs, and CLIs.** Agents also need thorough documentation to discover, sign up for, and start using new tools programmatically — without a human in the loop.

**The opportunity:** Every major category of software that people use today needs to be rebuilt for agents. The new agent-first software won't come from incumbents bolting on agent support — it'll come from startups that build explicitly for agents as first-class citizens.

**The meta-insight:** While everyone else is building agents, the biggest opportunity might be building the software those agents depend on: **"Make Something Agents Want."**

### 14. Startups That Want to Sell to Huge Companies

**By:** Harshita Arora and Brad Flora (Group Partners, Y Combinator)  
**Video:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Lrh1JVxqrKE

**The current state:** PG's classic advice: startups should sell to other startups — smart, forward-thinking users who help shape your product. But AI has opened a new frontier: **the Fortune 100.**

**The shift — three things changed:**
1. **Buyers are awake.** The people running the world's largest companies aren't hiding — they're actively seeking teams that can bend AI to solve key problems. In the last three years, YC companies have landed pilots and multimillion-dollar deals within their first year, sometimes during the actual YC batch. A company's first customer being one of the world's largest companies is no longer unusual.
2. **Small teams can ship fast.** AI has made it possible for 2–3 person teams to ship products large organizations find useful before the incorporation docs are dry. The "stealth for 3 years" model is dead.
3. **Leaders understand the stakes.** F100 executives know where their companies should create value internally, what they should outsource, and what happens if they don't adapt.

**The opportunity:** This is a terrific environment for founders to sell into — back more teams going after deals with the most famous, important customers in the world.

### 15. Supply Chain 2.0 for Semiconductors

**By:** Diana Hu (Group Partner, Y Combinator)  
**Video:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=lw6xcZ9uuuw

**The current state:** A single advanced AI chip goes through ~1,400 process steps, crosses a dozen countries, and takes five months to build. This supply chain is managed with spreadsheets, SAP, and phone calls. In 2021, a $300 chip held up a $50,000 car, and $210 billion in vehicles didn't get built. Companies could see their direct suppliers but had zero visibility into second and third tiers.

**It's gotten worse:** TSMC's advanced packaging is the single biggest bottleneck in AI compute. NVIDIA has locked up over 60% of it. HBM memory is booked through 2026. Export controls change quarterly. At the same time, the CHIPS Act is standing up new American fabs in Arizona, Texas, Ohio, and New York — each needing a supply chain built nearly from scratch.

**The gap:** Almost none of the tooling you'd expect exists: real-time allocation tracking, multi-tier risk monitoring, export compliance. You need deep understanding of wafer allocation and packaging constraints to build this — which is exactly why it's a startup opportunity and not a feature inside SAP.

### 16. The AI Operating System for Companies

**By:** Diana Hu (Group Partner, Y Combinator)  
**Video:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K46ZNekw2Tk

**The current state:** The best AI-native companies have figured out something most haven't: they've made their *entire company queryable* — every meeting recorded, every ticket tracked, every customer interaction captured, all legible to an intelligence layer that learns from it. This turns a company from an open loop (decide → check results weeks later) into a closed loop (system monitors → compares → adjusts). Teams that do this cut sprint time in half and ship twice as much.

**The problem:** Building this today requires brutal integration work — stitching together Slack, Linear, GitHub, Notion, call recordings, and a dozen other tools with custom glue code. There's no product that connects all this context into a single intelligence layer that can reason across it, flag when engineering is building the wrong thing, or generate specs agents can execute on.

**The opportunity:** Build the connective layer that makes a company legible to AI by default. Not another dashboard — the system that turns a company's own artifacts into a self-improving loop.

## How to Use RFS for Idea Generation

### 1. Read RFS as a Signal, Not a Menu

RFS entries represent what *smart investors with broad exposure* see as opportunities. They are not a list of "approved" ideas. The value is in understanding *why* these partners think this moment is different. The underlying analytical move in every RFS is the same: **identify the enabling condition that just flipped.**

When you read an RFS, ask:
- What changed? Why is this solvable *now* and not five years ago?
- What cost curve, technology, or regulation crossed a threshold?
- What assumption about the world no longer holds?

### 2. Look for Patterns Across Batches

YC has published multiple RFS batches (the page has tabs for Summer 2026, Spring 2026, Fall 2025, Summer 2025, Spring 2025, Winter 2025, Summer 2024). Recurring themes across batches signal *durable conviction* rather than a single partner's enthusiasm. When the same thesis appears across batches from different partners, pay attention.

### 3. Use RFS as a Pattern-Matching Tool

The most powerful use of RFS is not picking an idea from the list — it's using the list to calibrate your own pattern-matching. When you encounter a startup idea (your own or someone else's), ask: *Would this fit as an RFS? What's the enabling condition? What shift makes it possible now?*

If you can't articulate the "why now" with the clarity of an RFS entry, you may not understand the opportunity deeply enough.

### 4. Cross-Reference with Your Own Unfair Advantages

The best startup ideas sit at the intersection of:
- A genuine shift (what RFS captures)
- Your unique skills, insights, or access

An RFS may describe the opportunity, but *your* specific angle on it — the domain you know, the customer segment you understand, the technical approach you've prototyped — is what makes it executable. Use RFS to validate that the opportunity is real, then apply your own lens.

### 5. Understand the Meta-Themes

Analyzing the Summer 2026 batch reveals several cross-cutting themes:

**AI as Infrastructure, Not Feature.** Nearly every RFS assumes AI is the foundation, not a differentiator. The question isn't "should we use AI?" but "what gets rebuilt when AI is the default?"

**From Software to Services.** Multiple RFS entries (AI-Native Service Companies, SaaS Challengers, Company Brain, AI OS for Companies) point to the same insight: the unit of value is shifting from software tools to completed outcomes. Companies that *do the work* rather than *sell the tool* capture more value.

**Hardware Renaissance.** RFS entries span chips (inference for agent workflows, electronics in space), supply chains (semiconductor supply chain 2.0, hardware iteration speed), and physical-world AI (agriculture, drone defense). After a decade of pure-software dominance, hardware is back — but with a software-native mindset.

**Closing the Loop.** AI-Native Discovery Engines, AI OS for Companies, and Inference Chips for Agent Workflows all share a structural pattern: moving from open-loop systems (act, then check later) to closed-loop systems (act, measure, adjust, repeat continuously). The winning architectures are those that learn and self-correct.

**Defense as a Startup Category.** Counter-Swarm Defense explicitly frames defense not as a government procurement problem but as a real-time distributed systems problem — a domain where startup-speed iteration has structural advantage over traditional defense contractors.
